New coronavirus clinical trial will test repurposed drugs on patients in more than a dozen countries

Mark Johnson, Milwaukee Journal Sentinel Published 11:34 a.m. CT April 9, 2020 | Updated 4:54 p.m. CT April 9, 2020
CONNECTTWEETLINKEDINCOMMENTEMAILMORE

An international clinical trial combining treatment and research in a new way has started in Europe and will begin within days in the U.S., testing drugs approved for other uses to see if they help moderately and severely ill COVID-19 patients.

The project was announced Thursday morning by scientists at the University of Pittsburgh School of Medicine.

The new trial aims to find a middle ground between the urgency of a doctor treating a severely ill patient now and the rigor of a scientist collecting painstaking evidence from many patients over a long period.

“There are very formidable barriers to executing a clinical trial in the middle of a pandemic,” said Derek Angus, a member of the international steering committee guiding the new trial and chairman of the university's Department of Critical Care Medicine.

“Everyone on the planet is on COVID-19 time. Our lives have all changed.”

At the time of Thursday's announcement, the COVID-19 pandemic had infected about 1.5 million people worldwide, killing about 90,000.

Traditional trials test a single drug against a placebo, going through separate phases to prove that the drug is safe and effective.

The new trial, described in a paper in the journal Annals of the American Thoracic Society, will test multiple different treatments simultaneously, trying seven already-approved drugs in various combinations. Patients will be randomly assigned to one treatment; about 12.5% will serve as the control group receiving the best standard care.

As the trial proceeds, Angus said, algorithms will steer more and more patients toward the combinations that show the best results. At the other end of the spectrum, drug cocktails that appear ineffective will be phased out.

Angus compared the algorithms that researchers are using to those employed by companies like Google and Amazon when they choose advertising that is uniquely tailored to the individual typing at a computer.

The seven drugs in the study include steroids, medications that alter the immune system and hydroxychloroquine, an anti-malarial medication that has been touted by President Donald Trump. So far, there has been little solid evidence that hydroxychloroquine works. 

The new trial already involves more than a dozen countries.

Angus said the research that led to the trial design received significant funding from the European Union, which committed millions of euros to "Pandemic Planning" several years ago. He could not estimate the cost of the trial, but said that it is being borne by University of Pittsburgh Medical Center, which runs 40 hospitals and is affiliated with the school of medicine.

Angus said the U.S. Food and Drug Administration knows about the design of the new trial and has cleared the use of existing drugs.

The United Kingdom's four chief medical officers have sent a letter urging all hospitals in their countries to participate in three clinical trials; one is the trial devised by the team in Pittsburgh.

"The faster people are recruited to clinical trials and other studies, the sooner we will get the evidence so patients can get the best possible treatment," Chris Whitty, England's chief medical officer recently tweeted. "Those who volunteer for studies will help others in the future."

The trial has already begun in European countries, Australia and Canada. Angus said the first American patients will be enrolled in the coming days at the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center.

Thursday's announcement adds to a growing number of large national and international scientific efforts that have emerged to fight COVID-19, including one that involves using plasma from recovered patients to treat those still sick with the disease.

Plasma trials have received FDA approval and are already moving forward at medical centers including those affiliated with Johns Hopkins University and University of Wisconsin-Madison. The FDA has also approved the use of survivor plasma to treat patients on an experimental, compassionate basis.

The urgency of the COVID-19 pandemic has sped up the pace of new research reports significantly, raising concerns that the scientific community may be overpromising results and skirting its traditionally rigorous checks and balances.

"It's definitely one of the more novel way of testing therapies," said Hana El Sahly, an associate professor of molecular virology and microbiology at the Baylor College of Medicine.

She said she wasn't sure why researchers have chosen some of the medications they are testing, but said they have made the trial "user-friendly."

"They're going to have to recruit a lot of subjects, and the pandemic is raging, so there will be patients to recruit," she said

Kyungmann Kim, a professor in UW's department of biostatistics and medical informatics, said, "I hope the clinical community does not forget" that what he called "adaptively-designed trials" have on at least two occasions failed to provide adequate answers to the central questions they examined.  

The new trial will rely on electronic medical records and will gain patient consent by teleconference in order to observe social distancing.

Angus said the project will use a central database in Sydney, Australia, and a statistical center in Austin, Texas.

"It's a big change," Angus said of the trial he's helping to launch. "We're trying to change the world."
