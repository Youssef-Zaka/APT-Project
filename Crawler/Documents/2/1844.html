STEAM/PASS Program Hosts Media Presentation

By Annie Gentile
Courant Community |
Jun 27, 2018 at 1:06 PM

Grabbing a burger and fries at a fast food restaurant may be quick and easy, but a steady diet of such food isn't healthful.

Knowing what we put in our bodies and making smart choices is key to maintaining a healthy diet.

Advertisement

The same can be said of the media we consume. Getting our information from a variety of sources and knowing the sources of our information is important if we want to make informed decisions.

On June 14, the Stafford Elementary School STEAM/PASS program hosted 'Balancing Your Media Diet,' an adult educational forum presented by Adam Chiara, assistant professor of communication at the University of Hartford.

The media landscape has changed enormously in the past 35 years, starting in the 1980s when cable television with its accompanying 24/7 news cycle started making its way into living rooms across the country, Chiara said. Prior to that time, 75 percent of households watched network television news where the major stories of the day and the facts provided were largely the same.

There are only so many breaking stories, however, and cable news networks needed to find ways to fill 24 hours of airspace. This was the dawn of opinion news and the introduction of talking heads who offered differing and conflicting opinions on major stories.

The model of friction and tension worked, and coverage began to skew toward one set of opinions or another. In turn, viewers looking for confirmation of their own biases would tune into the network that most aligned with their opinions to satisfy their news diet.

"About two-thirds of Americans get at least some of their news from social media," added Chiara.

The problem with that venue is that people will often get news stories from what their friends share in their news feeds.

"The reason? It's a personal endorsement," said Chiara.

We get news from our friends who are similar to us, who share similar viewpoints.

People should understand that the stories that show up in their feeds are not random, said Chiara. For example, Facebook will show people stories they want to see based on an algorithm, a calculation based on the types of stories people choose to click on.

To counter the algorithm when reading any news article, people should be mindful and want to know who authored the piece, the source of the article, its purpose, and the quality of the information, Chiara said.

They should be aware of the techniques used to attract their attention, the lifestyle values and points of view that are being represented, how others might interpret the message, and what, if anything was omitted or downplayed in the story.

"We are creating our own alternate universe," said Chiara. "You get in your bubble. You become isolated in your silo."

However, by being discerning with our news diet, viewers are less likely to be susceptible to completely made up news stories - the true definition of "fake news."

"We live in the most amazing time in communication. We take for granted that we can take out our phones and instantly get news from China, from Los Angeles. We can listen to podcasts and watch videos," said Chiara. "But if every story you read just confirms your values, you may not be getting a mixed diet."

As a library media specialist for Stafford Elementary School, Rachael Funk said she grew up going to the library and using hard sources to do research.

"Now, kids go to the internet to do their research," she said.

The challenge for parents and teachers is to help them navigate the information they are getting, starting early on in elementary school and continuing through middle school and into high school.

"We have a whole curriculum based on this," said Funk. "Technology has grown in leaps and bounds and teachers are a bit behind the curve. There's a big piece we need to do so that kids don't make mistakes."
