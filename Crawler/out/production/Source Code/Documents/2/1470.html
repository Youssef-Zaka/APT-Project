Abstract

In this paper, I discuss the possible relations between Fritz London’s account of the status of the observer in quantum physics and transcendental phenomenology. Firstly, I discuss Steven French’s interpretation of London’s thesis as a phenomenological account of the status of the observer, along with the objections Otávio Bueno has brought forward. Secondly, refusing in part both French’s and Bueno’s theses for several reasons, I propose another way of reading London’s thesis in the framework of transcendental phenomenology. Namely, I put London’s account of the observer against the backdrop of Husserl’s analyses of the objectifying acts and objectual constitution. Finally, I end this article with some criticisms of what seems to me the ontological indigency of the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum physics, to whose “spirit” London also belongs.

Introducing the question

In 1939, Fritz London, a physicist with a philosophic gift, co-authored with Edmond Bauer a short monograph titled La théorie de la mesure en physique. Le problème de l’observation en physique quantique.Footnote 1 The monograph was a kind of digest of the hard, fundamental mathematical work done by von Neumann in his 1932’s Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik. There, the states of quantum systems were distinguished into two kinds. One, fundamental, that evolves in a time-continuous, reversible and causal way, modeled by the Schrödinger’s wave equation, which von Neumann called processes of kind 2, and another, of kind 1, that is characterized by a non-causal, time irreversible and instantaneous “collapse” of the quantum system into a definite value. Processes of kind 1 cannot be reduced to processes of kind 2. They are entirely different and there must be a discontinuous “cut-point.” Von Neumann located his theory of measurement (Messung) and of observation (Beobachtung) in the passage from kind 2 processes to those of 1, identifying therefore the act of measurement as the trigger of the two qualitatively incompatible evolutions of quantum systems. Von Neumann did not enter into great detail either about the status of the observation, or the point where it takes place. The cut between physical system evolving according to Schrödinger’s equation and measurement was rather, for him, a fluctuating one. Following this line of thought, it is generally accepted that London and Bauer suggested in their essay that the measurement by means of which an irreversible, definite value for a quantum system is obtained entails the intervention of an observation that is presented as a conscious act. Hence, consciousness was strangely called to play a decisive role in the supposedly most fundamental theory of physical reality. In so doing, they were considered proponents of a “mentalist” account of the measurement process and of the “collapse” of the wave function,Footnote 2 which reached its peak with Eugene Wigner’s interpretation. As this mentalist approach came to be severely criticized, London’s theory of the conscious observer has become no more than a reference mark, even a “peculiarity,” of a historically dismissed theory.

However, it has recently been suggested by Steven French that the observer problem in quantum physics, as London has put it, could be an acceptable response when viewed from a phenomenological perspective, namely through the vantage point of Husserl’s transcendental phenomenology.Footnote 3 In light of this philosophical theory and taking a step further, one could even claim that the riddle of observation in quantum physics would at last confirm the phenomenological general thesis that there is no being without a relation to consciousness. Thus, the being-relative to consciousness of whatever kind of entities, i.e., their status as beings only in the framework of a subjective constitution, as highlighted by the phenomenological principle of correlation, would seem to be the lesson one could learn from the observation problem in quantum physics. Hence, one could say that quantum physics presents, as an unsolved problem, the facts that eventually validate a philosophical theory regarding knowledge and the general status of reality, inasmuch as this theory could give the conceptual (and ontological) framework that makes these very facts understandable.

Indeed, in a stimulating paper, French states that the historical origins of the “orthodox solution”Footnote 4 to the measurement problem as proposed by London and Bauer in 1939 are to be found in transcendental phenomenology.Footnote 5 As external evidence, French refers Fritz London’s proximity to the Husserl’s circle via Pfänder in the early twenties, more specifically the publication in the Jahrbuch für Philosophie und phänomenologische Forschung, Vol. VI, 1923 (edited both by Pfänder and by Husserl himself, as editor-in-chief), of his Ph.D. thesis titled Über der Bedingungen der Möglichkeit einer deduktiven Theorie.Footnote 6

Clearly, London’s thesis can be viewed as dealing with an issue that belongs to the Husserlian domain of formal ontology. Moreover, in 1939’s monograph where he addresses the measuring problem, London, as French stresses, expressly refers to Husserl’s works in his concluding remarks.Footnote 7 This latter reference, which is made in the last paragraph and refers to the observer-thesis of the eleventh paragraph, amounts to internal evidence that London’s (and Bauer’s) solution of the measuring problem appeals to a supposed phenomenological account of the status of a conscious observer in quantum theory. Indeed, London and Bauer refer to a subjective capacity of self-awareness by introspection as a necessary condition to carry out an act of physical observation, a capacity which French states that they understood in “phenomenological terms.”Footnote 8 As London and Bauer expressly acknowledge in the forward, their endeavor was “to understand what modern physics says about the analysis of the act of observation by itself,” doing some foundational work because “physicists are to some extent sleepwalkers, who try to avoid such issues.”Footnote 9 Thus, the reference to Husserl’s phenomenology would be tantamount to something like putting a philosophical theory of the subjective constitution of the object of knowledge in the very heart of a quantum-physical account of the act of observation, construing the latter as a process of self-awareness by a conscious subject.

But things are not as solid as they seem concerning this supposed connection of transcendental theory of objective constitution with subjective capabilities for self-knowledge by introspection and observation in quantum physics. Appraising French’s paper, Bueno has a point when he stresses that it is one thing to assert the necessity of an observer for the so-called “collapse” of the wave function, and quite another to construe the observer as a consciousness that could no longer be accounted for as a physical entity. He emphasizes—rightly, I believe—that (i) the appeal to consciousness to account for the collapse of the wave function is not suggested by von Neumann, as is usually believedFootnote 10; that (ii) this status of consciousness in the act of observation was rather introduced by Wigner; and finally that (iii) London’s account of the role of the observer is not of such a kind, at least for the “minimalist interpretation” he suggests.Footnote 11 Indeed, Bueno construes London’s descriptions as an account of “what is involved in the acts of observation in quantum mechanics and the role played by the observer in them,” not as a solution of the measurement problem, which concerns how to “reconcile two incompatible evolutions of quantum systems.”Footnote 12 As a point of principle to his interpretation, Bueno remarks that London’s and Bauer’s account of the intricacies of observation in quantum theory is not as philosophically overloaded as to include a phenomenological conception of the subjective constitution of the object of knowledge. As a result, contrary to French’s account, his “minimalist interpretation” does not regard the “reference to ‘immanent knowledge’ as something that ought to be read phenomenologically.”Footnote 13

So, returning to French’s interpretation, one finds a triple claim. Namely,

  1. 1.

    That London was proposing a solution for the measurement problem in quantum physics by means of a theory of the observer and construing the concept of an observer as an entity endowed with special properties like consciousness and the capability of self-knowledge (by introspection or self-reflection). Bueno’s disagreement on the “solution-account” is a matter of the exegetical analysis of London’s essay.

  2. 2.

    That London’s account of the observer must be understood against the backdrop of the phenomenological theory of the subject (the “I”), evading this way the dualistic separation between matter and mind and the criticisms (by Putnam and Shimony) that were made on that basis. Bueno’s claim that London’s description of the observer is not philosophically overloaded and gives rise to such kind of criticisms is once more, but now only partially, a matter of exegetical analysis.

  3. 3.

    That, finally, what is known as the phenomenological theory of objective constitution (not “creation,” as the English translation of London’s essay misleadingly reads) is the key to understand the problem of measurement in quantum physics, overlapping the issues of objectual constitution by transcendental subjectivity, the capability for self-knowledge by “introspection” of an “I,” and the “mundane” (i.e., empirical) acts of observation by a specific human subject: the physicist him/herself (or one of Wigner’s friends…). This is a matter of open, philosophical discussion.

Thus, in what follows, I will start by addressing directly the first two issues. Concerning the third, my discussion, while being spread over the entire paper, will be concentrated in the final sections. I will argue firstly that London’s most fundamental epistemological issue is the objectivating act, sharply criticizing a naïve philosophical conception of objectivity (also prevailing among classical physicists) when he asserts that “the idea of an observable world, totally independent of the observer, [is] a vacuous idea.”Footnote 14 Then I will reappraise the thesis that London relies on phenomenology for his account of objectivity, disentangling this issue from the one about the mentalist conception of an observer endowed with a psychological faculty of self-reflection. I will finish with what is, in my view, the main contribution one can (and should) expect from a phenomenological, transcendental approach to these epistemological and philosophical issues. In my account, I will not conflate the theory of objectual constitution by a transcendental subject with the supposed dependence of the actual world on an actual psychological observer (a kind of Berkeleyan subjective idealism) and, above all, with an “influence” of consciousness on physical systems for triggering the collapse of the wave function, à la Wigner.Footnote 15 Needless to say: the expectations I expressed at the outset of a happy marriage between a mentalist account of the observer, within quantum physics, and the phenomenological principle of correlation, belonging to transcendental phenomenology, are not mine at all. In my opinion, the link between London’s phenomenologically inspired account of observation (I fully agree with French in this point) and quantum physics lies elsewhere than in the theory of the “I.”

What about observation? London’s quantum-physical account

What is truly the issue of London and Bauer 1939 essay, titled “Le problème de la mesure en physique,” and subtitled “La théorie de l’observation en mécanique quantique”?

Firstly, and undoubtedly, the establishment of a link between the measuring problem in quantum physics and the intervention of an observer. The immediate background is well-known: Born’s probabilistic interpretation of Schrödinger’s equation, the mathematical framework developed by von Neumann in his axiomatization of quantum physics, with his distinction between a deterministic, causal, time-continuous evolution of a quantum system in superposition, and the probabilistic, “non-causal,” “non-deterministic” and time-irreversible “collapse” of the wave-function (the “reduction” of the state-vector) into a single eigenstate corresponding to an eigenvalue of a measured quantity. Von Neumann’s “projection postulate” (a term coined later by H. Margenau) had an intra-theoretical, mathematical treatment and an extra-theoretical motivation: The verification that an observer never registers a superposition of states, but always a definite eigenvalue for a given observable. As von Neumann’s mathematical treatment considered the physical apparatus through which the measurement is made as a new quantum system on the same footing as the former, and also the body and the brain of the observer, entering all of them, as physical systems under the universal reach of quantum theory, into a global superposition, the door was opened for a mentalist account of the status of the observer capable of triggering the collapse of the wave-function. This was the path already anticipated by Charles Galton Darwin in 1929Footnote 16 and famously followed by Eugene Wigner in 1961,Footnote 17 among others.

Are London and Bauer going in the same direction, as we are usually told, so that, as in Wigner’s case, “the impression that one gets at an interaction, called also the result of an observation, modifies the wave function on the system”?Footnote 18 Does an observer always have to be something endowed with some psychological properties, and not simply a physical apparatus in order for a reduction occur?

Strangely enough, I find in their essay assertions that clearly go in the opposite direction, that is, assertions that, using von Neumann’s symbols “I” for the measured system, II for the measuring apparatus, and III for the observer (see von Neumann 1981, 234), put the measure act in I/II, and not in I + II/III. Indeed, in the last section of the essay, London and Bauer stress that “the act of observation, that is, the coupling between the measuring apparatus and the observer, is truly a macroscopic action and not one essentially quantal,” and they therefore conclude that, “one always has the right to neglect the effect on the apparatus of the look [regard] of the observer.” This neutrality of conscious observation, as a macroscopic event, concerning I/II, allows one “to return back in time” for obtaining “definite conclusions about the state of the apparatus […] and consequently the state of the object before the observation (but of course after their coupling).”Footnote 19 Earlier in the essay, in section 11, London and Bauer provided an example: the Stern-Gerlach apparatus for measuring the magnetic moment of a silver atom. In their mathematical treatment, before the interaction, the beam of atoms and the apparatus (a nonuniform magnetic field) are described as two pure cases with two proper statistical matrices and corresponding wave-functions. As they interact, that is, as the beam passes through the magnetic field and is split into several beams corresponding to the different values of k (− 1, 0, 1), the composite system is mathematically modeled as a mixture, where every component of each matrix is tied with the components of the other by a one-to-one correspondence, entailing the “associated correlations.”Footnote 20 Now, the point is that, in the following section, London and Bauer stress that “it is enough to make a suitable slit through the screen on which the atoms fall to transform the Stern-Gerlach apparatus into a source of identically oriented atoms.” Thus, the Stern-Gerlach apparatus through which the beam of atoms passes, i.e., I and II alone, “becomes a good set-up for producing pure cases,” inasmuch as every single one of the filtered atoms has now a corresponding wave-function of its own.Footnote 21 In a nutshell, in the mathematical description, the quantum system progresses from a pure case to a mixture and then to a set of new pure cases without the intervention of a psychological observer. Indeed, the set {apparatus + screen} counts as an observer in a pure physical sense: it triggers the so-called “collapse” of the wave function into a set of several eigenstates.

If this is right, Bueno has a point when he claims that London was not providing a solution to the measurement problem by his theory of the psychological observer. Definitively, London’s approach is not a variant of the “conscience causes collapse” theory. What is thus the contribution of the observer as a psychological subject? London and Bauer are very clear about that. It regards not the production of quantum physical phenomena but instead the constitution of knowledge about them. Taken seriously the concepts that London uses here, this move must be interpreted phenomenologically, because the production of knowledge is described as a subjective objectifying act that is constitutive of a new state-of-affairs (Sachverhalt). These are, indeed, strong phenomenological concepts. Thus there is truly a productivity springing from the conscious observer. However, rather than physically producing a pure case, it constitutes a new objectivity as correlated with a new piece of knowledge: the fact that the quantum system (and the apparatus, correspondingly) is in such and such a state.

Indeed, London and Bauer expressly state that there is no contradiction in his description of the Stern-Gerlach measurement (which is just an example) and their account in section 11 of the act of objectivation, accomplished by an observer’s effective intervention, to establish a pure case. Their explanation runs as follows: (1) For the atoms that went through the slit, the physicist can attribute to each of them the wave function of a pure case; (2) Nevertheless, this is done at the expense of the individuality of the object, inasmuch as the physicist does not “know which are the atoms that have the property in question.” Consequently, “we can easily attribute to the objects that get through the slit the ψ function of the pure case, but we cannot say which object, that is, which variable is the argument of this ψ function.” This is tantamount to concluding that “the filter alone produces pure cases, but in an absolutely anonymous form, [which] is different from a true measurement,”Footnote 22 that is, according to my rendering, one that produces new cognitive information about the system inasmuch as it constitutes a definite state-of-affairs. Indeed, what one has before the conscious observation is only a probability distribution of pure cases regarding the magnetic moment of the silver atoms. However, this is not an entangled state, where one finds non-classical probabilities. On the contrary, the probabilities are here entirely classical: One knows in advance that the beam of silver atoms passed through the magnetic field and dispersed in a set of beams according to the possible values of k. Once, one can calculate global value-distributions, and the probabilities regard here only one’s ignorance about the details of the system. Notwithstanding, even when a “true” observation takes place, involving an act of direct taken notice of the system (instead of only mathematically calculating the probability distribution of the results), quantum physics always remains, as London and Bauer remark, a “theory of species” because it is never an individual entity that becomes known (given the statistical form of the theory). So the very principle equating objectivity and individuality must be revised, even when cognitive information is produced about the quantum system by a subject of knowledge (an “observer”).

Where lie, then, the conditions for a “true measurement”? Of course, I am referring to a measurement that is not described as the process {pure case—mixture—new unknown pure case}, but instead as an objectifying act conducting to an increase of cognitive information about the physical system itself, insofar as the objectifying act constitutes (not: “creates”) a determinate pure case by finding the argument of the (already existing) new ψ function.

This is tantamount to asking for the theory of knowledge (if any) that underlies London’s account of the status of the conscious observer.

The cognitive import of the “true” observation

It is worth noting that, in the famous section eleven where London and Bauer describe the “true” observational act, it is presented as an “increase of knowledge” fulfilling the growth of uncertainty coming from an “incomplete knowledge” of the composite system formed by the object and the apparatus.Footnote 23 Instead of creating a new pure case (which is a quantum physical event), the “true” act of observation constitutes a new object (which is an epistemic-dependent reality) by means of a corresponding objectifying act: the determination of a new value for the ψ function.

Thus the role of the conscious observer seems to be wholly epistemic, as the very title of section eleven announces: “Mesure et observation. L’acte d’objectivation.”Footnote 24 Indeed, the concept of an objectifying act is doubtless something that must be read in the framework of Husserl’s phenomenological concept of the objektivierender Akt and his theory of knowledge and objectual constitution (Konstitution). Moreover, together with the concept of an “objectifying act,” the word “constitution” (constitution, in London’s French text) expresses a concept that is at the very heart of the phenomenological theory.Footnote 25 Certainly, it is not by pure chance that London choses to use these so phenomenologically overload expressions. If this is proven right, Bueno’s deflationary interpretation of the role of the observer, according to which it is philosophically neutral, would turn out to be an incorrect one. However, slightly differing from French’s interpretation, the center of gravity of London’s phenomenological account of the act of true measurement would not be a phenomenological description of the “separation” of the subject from the physical superposition, but rather the outline of a transcendental analysis of the structure of the acts of objectivation as constitutive of states-of-affairs correlated with them. Indeed, section eleven is quite clear in what concerns, firstly, the act of observation and, then, the act of objectivation, by which a new state-of-affairs (correspondingly: a new knowledge of the physical system) is constituted. Though, contrary to French’s rendering, I believe that reflective consciousness in observation and the objectifying act (with the corresponding objectual constitution) are to be distinguished, instead of being conflated, because they have a different status.

To clarify these points, let me take stock.

To begin with, let us look at the overall theoretical context. London and Bauer successively explain that, (i) the ψ function has an objective character and encodes a complete knowledge of the physical systemFootnote 26; yet, (ii), by itself, it does not entail probabilities, but rather a “causal,” deterministic description of the evolution of the physical system over timeFootnote 27; so, (iii), if probabilities are to be introduced in the ψ function, then they are only potential rather than actual, and are dependent on the choice of a possible measure of a definite physical quantityFootnote 28; finally (iv) probabilities in quantum physics are not “classical,” because they do not express an incomplete knowledge of the system (it is “maximum” when the ψ function is given) but its possible relation to a measuring apparatus when a given property is selected, a property that only becomes actual in the very process of measuring it.Footnote 29

Thus, relying on this account, one could remark that what is measured is not something already present in the system, but rather the outcome of its interaction with a given apparatus. London emphasizes this point. It could be stated as the non-backwards projection of the observed properties over the physical system before or without measurement. He expressly presents this impossibility as a non-classical feature of quantum mechanics. So, given this “invisibility” of the physical systems without measurement or observation, they are like a black box that cannot be even represented by concepts with an ontological import. Unlike the wave functions of optics (which London presents as an analogy) that model electromagnetic radiation such as light, the ψ function does not have an identifiable reality to which it applies. From here comes the tendency to blend the ψ function and the entity it models, reifying the mathematical model and taking it for the reality itself. Therefore, a second remark here in order is that one can raise an in principle doubt regarding the tendency to conflate the ψ function, which is a mathematical object that models the state of a supposed physical entity, and the very physical systems it intends to describe—No one would say that Newton’s gravitational law, F = GM1m2/d2, is the physical systems to which it applies; however, for ponderous reasons, this clear distinction becomes unclear in quantum physics.

Returning now to London’s and Bauer’s explanations, an embarrassing question is unavoidable, as they expressly emphasize: if a measuring and an observation are to be made and a definite result obtained therein, “by what right can we add this new knowledge to the supposedly complete knowledge that we already had?”Footnote 30 London’s response is straightforward: Heisenberg discovered that the very process of measuring produces indeterminacy in the state of the object, as the coupling of the system with the apparatus introduces a statistical configuration of the whole formed by the two systems (a mixture) and a loss of knowledge about them. This is the origin of probabilities in the classical sense, which the measurement process should, then, lead to a fixed value in order to overcome the now incomplete knowledge of the whole system.

Nevertheless, I believe that London and Bauer avoid the central issue of their own question: Why should an observational act be accomplished at all, if we already are in full possession of a supposedly complete knowledge of the system? In a nutshell, what lack does the observational act intend to fulfill (creating by itself statistical probabilities that must be determined)? Certainly, they take for granted the fact that any scientific theory must be confirmed by experimental tests of its own previsions. So, experimental testing shows an overall lack of any scientific theory, insofar as every theory, to be a scientific one, must always be put to the test. Measurement and observation constitute the link between theory and experience. Because it is obvious, this is perhaps the reason for their silence. Nevertheless, I believe that there is a hidden and deeper reason. Indeed, a quantum theory must first prove the very reality of the objects it posits, because it is a physics of invisible entities that cannot even be presented in some pictorial representation in space and time. So I am inclined to say that the most fundamental lack in quantum theory, which calls for observation and measurement, is the lack of reality, because the system mathematically described by the ψ function alone is a “ghost-world” not only pictorially irrepresentable, but also a world where nothing effectively happens. Indeed, a world without definite events does not fulfill the necessary conditions to be posited as a world in the pregnant sense.Footnote 31 Actually, the event-structure that belongs to the sense of a world is only constituted by a definite value of the system in the framework of macroscopic, space–time coordinates, precisely the coordinates where the physicist performs his acts of observation. These definite values as observed in macroscopic coordinates (which refer back to the lifeworld of the scientist) are the elements that provide the necessary conditions for the constitution of something as actual instead of merely potential. Therefore, I believe that the sheer quantum mathematical approach is by itself incapable of producing proof of its objects. Hence, the lack it has is not only a lack of experimental testing for the previsions made, but a deeper lack of evidential proof of the very “mathematical-physical” entities it puts forth (I will come to this issue later).

One is now fit to appraise London’s and Bauer’s description of the act of observation and the corresponding objectifying act. In the framework of the mathematical treatment by von Neumann, the eleventh section of the essay provides both a mathematical and an observational, extra-mathematical account of the act of measuring. From a mathematical point of view (which London’s calls “objective”), for a physical quantity F and the coordinates G to fixate the possible positions of the pointer of the measuring device, the physical system and the apparatus, before the coupling, are altogether described by the function Ψ(x,y) = ν0(y) \(\sum\nolimits_{k} \psi k\;uk\left( x \right)\). After the coupling, a new encompassing wave function, describing the whole system, obtains: Ψ(x,y) = \(\sum\nolimits_{k\rho } {\varPsi } k\rho uk\left( x \right) \nu \rho \left( y \right)\). Now, when the FF are coordinate with the GG, to not only having a coupling but also a measurement, there emerges a state “having for each separated system, object and apparatus, the character of a mixture, […] and a correlation between the two mixtures, namely, the certainty that, if G = gk, then F = fk.”Footnote 32 The point is that, mathematically, one can only establish the correlations, but not decide what the value for G is and, correspondingly, the value of F: “But of course, quantum mechanics does not allow us to predict which value actually be found in the measurement—the interaction with the apparatus does not conduct the object to a new pure stateFootnote 33 […]; [it] actually conduces to nothing more than a statistical mixture for the object and apparatus, i.e., an uncertainty coming from an incomplete knowledge.”Footnote 34

Thus, it is necessary to introduce extra-mathematical information to the knowledge the physicist has, and this entails that (i) the physicist knows in advance that a new pure case is produced by the interaction, so that there is a definite value of F to find out; that (ii) the source for obtaining information of the new pure case is perceptual observation (or other) at a macroscopic level (seeing G’s pointer, for instance); and that (iii) only a cognitive subject with the capacity to being aware of its perceptual states can fulfill the required conditions to join extra-mathematical information to the mathematical statistic mixture, overcoming the state of incomplete knowledge (s)he is in.

I believe this is precisely London’s line of reasoning: facing the “uncertainty coming from an incomplete knowledge,” “nothing prevents us from reducing it [that is, the incompleteness of our mathematical knowledge] by means of a later observation.” And “here is one’s opportunity.” By observing the pointer’s position (there is a definite position already), one gets an extra-mathematical “enrichment of knowledge, obtained by virtue of the observation, which gives the observer the right to choose among the different components of the mixture predicted by the theory¸ rejecting those that were not observed, and to attribute thenceforward to the object a new wave function, that of the pure case that he has found.”Footnote 35

The “mysterious” role of the observer boils down to a distinction between the treatment it receives within the mathematical formalism, where, following von Neumann, it is considered as nothing more than a new component of the mixture, and as an extra-mathematical source of knowledge by means of perception and the awareness of it. Mathematical quantum formalism spreads the knowledge into a set of probabilities for the apparatus and the physical system, including the possible perceptual states of the observer; it is a system of possible, but non-actual singular events where every case is predicted, but where nothing really happens. However, the concrete perception of the observer, not as it is calculated but as it is performed in the first-person perspective, by its very cognitive structure, is directed towards a single, actual and transcendent macroscopic worldly event. The mathematical subject and the observational subject are the very same. The issue regards how to join (or not) the independent information it possesses both as a subject of physical–mathematical knowledge and as a subject perceptually acquainted with a worldly event. In a nutshell, the deep phenomenological question would be a transcendental theory of the link between a physical–mathematical and a perceptual intentionality as constitutive of the identity and being of a quantum object, together with a transcendental account of the modes of giveness of such objects, i.e., of the way they can appear as being-there.

Of course, London’s explanations only suggest these deep phenomenological tasks. However, they are unavoidable when his outline of a theory of measurement is heard with phenomenological ears, as it must be. In his account, he distinguishes the “objective” from the “subjective” standpoint. If the physicist, albeit always being the subject of knowledge, treats him/herself as another object, then (s)he has, for him/her, the subject of knowledge, only a more complex mixture and a more complex mathematical formalism: {system + apparatus + observer}. However, if the physicist adds to the objective mathematical-physical mixture the extra-mathematical, perceptual knowledge (s)he has, then (s)he can use the perceptual knowledge to make a decision about the statistical probabilities (s)he got. The baffling situation is that the physicist is always the subject of knowledge, so that, in point of fact, London’s opposition between “objective” and “subjective” considerations misses the point. The true opposition is not between being either a subject for the world or an object in the world. In fact, the true opposition amounts to putting the knower in the mixture and being encapsulated in the statistics it provides, or (removing or not the observer from the mixture, because it is irrelevant whether the mixture has two or three components), to consider the knower as the source of another type of knowledge about the system. In so doing, the mathematical-physical system becomes then an object from which the physicist “externalizes” or “retreats” itself in order to retrieve new, perceptual knowledge of it.

Let us hear Fritz Wolfgang London himself:

The observer has a completely different standpoint [point de vue]: for him, it is only the object x and the apparatus y that belong to the external world, to what he calls ‘‘objective.’’ On the other hand, he has with himself relations of a very special character: he possesses a characteristic and quite familiar faculty which we can call the ‘‘faculty of introspection.’’ He can immediately be aware [se rendre compte] of his own state. It is by virtue of this “immanent knowledge” that he attributes the right to create his own objectivity, that is to say to cut the chain of statistical correlations, which is expressed by \(\mathop \sum \limits_{\text{k}} \psi kuk\left( x \right)\nu k\left( y \right)wk\left( z \right)\), by noticing [en constatant]: ‘‘I am in the state wk,” or more simply, ‘‘I see G = gk’’ or even directly, ‘‘F = fk.”Footnote 36

Accordingly, we will label this constitutive action as “making objective.” By it, the observer establishes his own framework of objectivity and acquires a new piece of knowledge about the object in question.Footnote 37

There is no trace here of a mysterious interference of consciousness in the outcome of the interaction between the physical system and the apparatus (or even with the physicist, taken as another physical component of the whole system). French is quite right when he emphasizes this aspect, stressing that the intervention of the observer does not cause the reduction of the wave function.Footnote 38 It is definitely not because the observer finds himself in state wk that the apparatus goes to state gk and the system to state fk. This avoids Putnam’s and Shimony’s objections. Quite the opposite, it is because the observer is a psychophysical subject that has internal states and a reflective awareness of them, that (i) (s)he can obtain definite information through immanent knowledge (say: “I now have a quite determinate sensation of a pointer’s spatial orientation: wk”), then (ii) constitute a new perceptual state-of-affairs (let us say: “the pointer of the apparatus indicates the definite value gk”), and finally (iii) constitute a new quantum-physical state-of-affairs for the system under study (“among the statistical probabilities predicted by the mathematical calculations over the mixture, the wave function collapsed into the eigenstate fk”).

However, contrary to French’s interpretation, the phenomenological background does not appear in London’s description of the observer’s “I.” It is rather a quite trivial account, and Bueno’s is right when he finds no more than a simple description of what an observer is and does. There is no question of understanding this “I” in light of Husserl’s account of the transcendental “I.” It is simply a psychophysical entity, the very human physicist, who, as a psychophysical subject, can have perceptual information and join it to the mathematical-physical calculations he does. Indeed, it is not the transcendental I that performs the acts of observation, as French himself recognizes when he writes that “the reflection that takes place in the measurement situation is not itself a phenomenological act, in the sense that one must first undertake the epoché in order to perform it.”Footnote 39 Being so, the reference to “immanent knowledge” and “introspection” points to capabilities of the subject as a mundane and not a transcendental one.Footnote 40 Thus any phenomenological account of the self-constitution of the transcendental subjectivity and of its self-apperception as a mundane subject is useless for what is currently at stake. Instead, what I believe must be read against a phenomenological background is London’s theses concerning the constitution of new objectivities by means of objectifying acts, be them perceptual, physical–mathematical, or, as is the case, sense-giving and constituting acts that blend them together. Moreover, contrary to what French takes for granted, the objectifying acts that constitute new objectivities are not a function of reflection. The act of objectual constitution is not a reflective act (be it a psychological or a transcendental reflection). Instead, it is straightforwardly directed towards its intentional object, although the phenomenological description of it is, in fact, an act of (transcendental) reflection.Footnote 41

Thus, in my opinion, rather than in the act of reflection, i.e., in the physicist’s awareness of his/her own states as a psychophysical “I,” the doctrine of objectual constitution by means of objectifying acts is the very point where London’s account meets phenomenology.

Accordingly, following French’s accurate hermeneutic insight concerning the relevance of phenomenology, I will try to show how the theory of constitution provides the right backdrop for London’s account of the status of the observer.

London’s philosophical agenda and the theory of objectifying acts

London writes as a physicist who has deep philosophical interests. From that stance, he directs some ultimate questions to philosophers, emphasizing that his own contribution is rather negative, but not an unimportant one because of that. As he stresses, “one can doubt the possibility of establishing philosophical truths by the methods of physicists, but it is surely not outside the competence of physicists to demonstrate that certain statements which pretend to have a philosophical validity do not—and sometimes these ‘negative’ philosophical discoveries by physicists are no less important, no less revolutionary for philosophy than the discoveries of professional philosophers.”Footnote 42

What then are London’s ultimate philosophical issues?

He provides a clear formulation of what is his most general goal: “To determine the necessary and sufficient conditions for an object of thought to be endowed with ‘objectivity,’ that is to say, to become object of science.”Footnote 43 This is so because the new developments of physics went beyond the classical idea of a “material object” and showed that “it is not true that the ‘objectivity’ of an object should be granted by the formal possibility of attributing to it in a continuous manner its measurable properties at the times in which it is not submitted to an observation.”Footnote 44

Briefly addressing the philosophical issue, London refers to the works of Malebranche, Leibniz, and Bolzano, both as mathematicians and as philosophers. After that, he remarks that Husserl himself created a new research method called “phenomenology” for dealing with these matters. In the corresponding footnote, he mentions Husserl’s Logische Untersuchungen and Ideen zu einer reinen Phänomenologie und phänomenologischen Philosophie: Erstes Buch, but also, important enough, Cassirer’s works Substanzbegriff und Funktionsbegriff and Determinismus und Indeterminismus in der modernen Physik.

Apparently these are rather disparate references. Is there a common denominator between Cassirer and Husserl? Certainly there is one: their endeavor to deal with the problem of objectivity from a transcendental stance, be it neo-Kantian or phenomenological. Yet is there a common background for Malebranche, Leibniz and Bolzano, as mathematicians and philosophers? At first glance, there is none. However, on second thought, a major issue comes up: the status of the infinite and of the continuous as mathematical objects and the ultimate structure of reality, that is, the doctrine of the continuous as the link and the point of unity between mathematics and physics. Indeed, albeit the difference between their doctrines, this is a common issue for the later MalebrancheFootnote 45 and for Leibniz, while Bolzano dealt with the problems of the infinite from a purely mathematical standpoint.Footnote 46 The mathematical treatment of the continuous is definitely the basis for introducing exact and formal essences that will then embed the objects given through perception, so that the perceptual giveness is taken as an appearance of a deep mathematical structure physically interpreted. This is the very core of the “substructions” (Husserl’s word) that give rise to modern physics. Furthermore, this is the point where ultimate and non-classical new issues come up in quantum physics. Thus if London’s references are coherent rather than disparate, they suggest that his deep concern was about the mathematical treatment of exact and formal essences as a framework for the science of nature, delivering a transcendental account of the way an “object of thought” can be endowed with objectivity and then become an object of (natural) science.

Hence, let us start with the phenomenological doctrine of objectifying acts. Being a transcendental query, it entails as a necessary condition the performance of the transcendental epoché.Footnote 47 In turn, as the procedure that gives access to the transcendental domain in which issues about objectivity are to be dealt with, the epoché can be presented as the transition from

  1. 1.

    A

as the existence of a certain entity of whatever ontological kind (a tree, a set, or the physical “thing” to which the ψ function applies), to

  1. 2.

    [A]

where the existence of A (and the knowledge one has of it) is put into brackets, i.e., not considered as the thematic field of research. This bracketing entails a backward disclosure of the processes in which [A] is intended, posited and eventually given. So, [A] becomes an objectual nucleus of sense (Sinn) and validity (Geltung), and no longer a “thing,” even though it corresponds to a thing posited in the natural attitude: precisely the A. Therefore, transcendental enquiry is about a structure of sense (the noema) to which corresponds a certain multiplicity of intentional acts (the noesis) whose sense-giving content and doxic character shall be descriptively exhibited. In these analyses, [A] functions as a “transcendental leading-clue.”

Now, the overall claim of phenomenology is that this procedure can be universalized, so that, for every X, there is the possibility of a bracketing, the epoché, that converts it into [X] and regressively describes the sense-giving, the sense-fulfilling and the doxic acts in which [X] is meant, posited and given as an object for consciousness.Footnote 48 This is stated as the principle of the noetic-noematic correlation. Thus, Husserlian phenomenology is committed to the principle:

  1. 3.

    There is no [X] without a dependence-relation to an (actual) consciousness

However, it does not follow from 3 that

  1. 3’.

    There is no X without a dependence-relation to an (actual) consciousness,

because, here, X is no longer a unity of sense and validity, but a thing taken as a mundane entity, and “consciousness” does not refer to the transcendental but instead to the psychological subjectivity. Thus, converting the transcendental dependence law into a mundane law, according to which every being depends on actually being “observed” or “noticed” by a conscious mundane subject, would entail a true metabasis eis allo genos and a kind of Berkeleyan idealism. Husserl says emphatically: “We, human beings, do not constitute the world—to affirm so would be a countersense.”Footnote 49 Indeed, the very sense of a mundane object, given by the natural attitude of experience, precisely amounts to being independent of its being-known and being-given to any mundane subject. This is, in fact, the meaning of removing the brackets and leaving the transcendental realm.Footnote 50 This undermines—only if I am right, no need to say—the overall strategy of making phenomenology productive for quantum theory from the observer’s side by establishing a dependence (supposedly phenomenologically construed) of any kind of being on a consciousness subject and particularly on a transcendental “I.”

Surely one could say that transcendental analyses would be then useless, because they will be unable to make statements about reality as such. However, it is quite the opposite. Transcendental analyses put to the fore the configurations of sense that are hidden in our natural experience of objects. Therefore they overcome the naiveté of the natural attitude, insofar as it simply takes for granted that there is a world that is experienced, without reflectively asking for the subjective conditions that make possible an experience of the world. In so doing, transcendental analyses do not preclude or substitute the work of positive sciences. Instead, they show how and by which sense configurations there is a realm of positive being for the corresponding positive research. Therefore, the gains of transcendental analyses are double fold. Firstly, they put forward some normative constraints regarding the several domains of objectivity, inasmuch as they determine what essentially pertains to their sense as a region of being, together with the other strata of sense that partially overlap, are founded upon or are foundations for it. Secondly, they put to the fore a strong condition to accepting the existence of something as being. As a general rule, as that stated in 3, the existence of a being X can only be acceptable if the corresponding [X] can be traced back to the subjective acts in which it is meant and given as an object, so that, for every X whose sense cannot be refereed back to a traceable subjective process that constitutes it as object (roughly, some kind of well-formed “construction”), X does not count as a true being in the pregnant sense. Thus, given what is at stake here, the clarification of sense foundation relations and sense crossing relations for the constitution of objectivities, together with the subjective, transcendental condition for something to be posited as being, are what one can expect of a transcendental theory of objectifying acts and objectual constitution.

In general, as Husserl has put it in the fifth logical investigation, an objectifying act (OA), being the fundamental form of intentionality, can be presented as

$${\text{I}}{-}OA =_{\text{def}} \left( {Q + M} \right) \to Ob$$

where Q is the quality of the act (Qualität), M its sense (intentionalen Materie), and Ob the object which is meant by and through the act.Footnote 51 The central point regarding what defines the objectifying quality (Q) of these acts is that they are positing acts, i.e., they range over the several modalities of belief (from simple belief to denial, moving through probability, likelihood, doubtfulness, and so on), with the corresponding modalities of being posited (as existing, as possible or probable being, as non-existent, and so on). The intentional matter (M) is construed as an “animation” of a given non-intentional content (primärer Inhalt) by a sense-bestowing act, which is accounted for as an interpretation (Deutung) or an apprehension (Auffasung). While Husserl’s basic model is the sense-animation of a sensuous content, his account can be widely construed as entailing that (i) there is a freedom in the bestowing of sense, and (ii) the constrains for selecting a given sense have to do with fitness with what is already given as a basis and general coherence with the other senses that belong to the same intentional realm. In a nutshell, if sense-bestowing is an interpretation (Deutung), this interpretation is regulated by relevance and coherence constraints. Regarding the object intentionally meant and perhaps also given in a fulfilling intuitive act, for the Husserl of the Logical Investigations it is an ordinary object of the world: a tree, a planet, but also a number, a function, a mathematical equation or another type of ideal objects.

Thus London’s reference to the objectifying acts in a phenomenological context means that there is an object only as a result of a sense-given act, so that it is the knowing subject that “establishes his own framework of objectivity and acquires a new piece of information about the object in question.”Footnote 52 Being the result of a constitutive process, one can say that the object was not already there before the very act. However, read phenomenologically, there is nothing mysterious regarding this intervention of a conscious observer. Indeed, (s)he does not create the reality s(he) observes. Rather, based on what is perceptually given, (s)he constitutes a new object of knowledge (In London’s example: F = Fk) in a “framework of objectivity” determined by the theoretical presuppositions that guided the relevant sense-bestowing and positing acts (in London’s example, the mixture formed by the ψ functions of the physical system and the apparatus).

Objectual constitution and the status of quantum physical entities

However, for London’s account of the status of objectivity, things start to get more interesting when one reaches the doctrine of constitution. Constitution (Konstitution) is a technical Husserlian term that refers not simply to the forms of intentionality but also to the realm of a phenomenology of reason.Footnote 53 The latter as to do with a theory of knowledge, as it exhibits the grounds for justifying the several positings of being. It was firstly developed in the final sections of Ideas-I and incorporates a more fine-grained analysis of the layers and elements of intentionality. To put them together in a single formula, one could write that an act of objectual constitution (OC) involves:

$${\text{II}}{-}OC =_{\text{def}} \{ (n \leftrightarrow N) \to \left( {\left[ {Ob_{w} } \right] \, \;\& \; \, \left( {H + O} \right) \, \;\& \;E} \right)\} \to Ob_{s}$$

Here n and N are the elements of the noetic-noematic correlation. H is the horizon of co-givenness for every object, which is an important addition Husserl made to his intentional theory.Footnote 54 The object as it is meant, which Husserl names the object in the how (Wie) of its determination, is signaled as Obw, to which corresponds the object simpliciter (Gegenstand schlechthin) which is intended: Obs. Despite these refinements to the former doctrine of the objectifying acts as the fundamental form of intentionality, the most important features are related to the constitutive theory in the framework of a phenomenology of reason. They include factors O and E, i.e., the set of formal and regional ontologies, with its relations of foundation and correlation, and the grounds of justification, E, for the doxic acts (belief-acts) and their modifications (doubtfulness, likelihood, and so on) that underpin the several positings of being. Thus, the doctrine of objectual constitution appeals to an evidential factor, E, and to an ontological framework, O (encompassing formal and material ontologies), for a phenomenological theory of justified positings as a theory of reason and reality.

Looking from this phenomenological stance at London’s theses about the status of objectivity, the relevant issues concern the sense-content of the acts and the grounds of justification for the corresponding postings, along with the set of ontologies the objectual constitution of quantum physical objects is spread over.

The latter issue has an obvious response. Constitution of quantum physical objects is related to the regional ontology of nature and, in addition, involves a heavy group of elements pertaining to formal mathematics (formal essences) and time and space idealizations (exact essences) as a four dimensional continuum where the “observables” of the theory are to be encountered. Nevertheless, things are not as simple. Firstly, there are representational links that join or not objects of each region. Indeed, not ever formal-mathematical object of the theory represents some thing or event in the region nature, i.e., a traceable feature of “our world,” so that there is no one-to-one correspondence, but a more complex net of representational and non-representational relations. Secondly, the region nature cannot be accounted for as simply being the registered observables of the theory. This would be a poor account of the sense content of the region nature as such. Indeed, the observables are embedded in a surrounding objectual horizon and are dependent on it. In this regard, Bell famously distinguished between the “observables” and the “beables” (substituting observ-able by being) of quantum theory, pointing out that the latter “must include the settings of switches and knobs on experimental equipment, the currents in coils, and the reading of instruments—‘observables’ must be made, somehow, out of beables.”Footnote 55 He characterized them as elements that must be described in “classical terms” and, for those that are “local,” assigned to “some bounded space–time region.”Footnote 56 Taking a step further, this is not just a case of translating the quantum physical results into “macroscopic” entities and in a language suited for doing it (here, in Bell’s account, a language that already depends on idealized exact essences of time and space and of space–time, as the four-vector of relativity). From a phenomenological standpoint, Bell’s important point is that observables suppose a horizon of technical devices and apparatuses which are accessible in a surrounding world constituted by perceptual acts. These perceptual acts open a more encompassing horizon of objects of every-day-life which include entities whose sense is determined by the regional ontology of nature: trees, planets, rivers, hurricanes, and so forth. The “observables” of the theory must be accounted for as features of this encompassing natural world, which refers back to a perceptual constitution of the ordinary objects of experience and to the language suited for expressing them. This does not mean that the results of quantum measures are to be expressed in every-day language. Of course, they cannot be expressed in ordinary language without losing their theoretical meaning. What it means is that these observables appear and are features of a surrounding natural world that can be referred to by pre-theoretical meaning-formations and the corresponding acts of expression. Indeed, only when they appear as a feature of the natural world can they be simultaneously interpreted by a sense-bestowing act which gives them a quantum physical sense according to the constraints of the theory. This interpretation functions as a backward “proof” of the existence of the mathema-physical objects posited by the theory. Moreover, at a closer look, these objectual sense-formations do not simply enter the regional ontology of nature. Indeed, for a complete exhibition of their horizons of sense, the social world must also be included into the set of relevant ontologies. To begin with, they must be referred to the “community of scientists” that constitutes a kind of “collective scientific consciousness,” as London and Bauer expressly remark in the last section of their essay, titled “Communauté scientifique et objectivité.”Footnote 57 Then, as their ultimate horizon, they must be set against the backdrop of the social and historical lifeworld to which these communities belong. A complete phenomenological theory of constitution, which explores the relevant ontologies and is guided by the concept of horizon, should be spread over this triplicity of formal and material ontologies.

Now, regarding sense and forms of justification, the ψ function is certainly an objectual sense-content (it belongs to the Husserlian noema), but by itself it does not have a representational link to objects of the region nature. Indeed, it does not refer to a traceable feature of the natural world. It is not a “beable,” as Bell puts it. So, it has no conditions of givenness. Further, it also does not refer to something in the global structure of the world when inserted in Schrödinger’s equation, because the latter cannot be construed as a wave’s time-continuous evolution in a real physical space. Indeed, it “evolves” in a configuration space, which is an object given by mathematical thinking alone, and Born’s rule presents it as simply expressing a probability density, |<λi|ψ>|2. The ψ function is then a defining formula (not a description of something) that encapsulates the quantum state of an isolated system. In this sense, it is complete: an isolated quantum system is how the ψ function defines it and nothing more. Despite lacking a representational link, as an object of pure thought it arguably has the kind of objectivity that, as London claims, is a necessary condition to become object of science. Firstly, it determines in advance the kind of observable results one can further obtain and their probability distribution by means of strictly regulated mathematical procedures defining what is valid or invalid, derivable or not derivable. In a nutshell, it is an object of predications and mathematical operations that can be true or false. Secondly, encoding all the information that can be additionally obtained of a quantum system, it is not posited as a sheer mathematical object. It is rather posited as the whole of possibilities for a physical system, and this whole is put, as such, as a state (a “superposition”), so that with it one posits not a definite being, but instead the effectiveness of the complete set of real possibilities of a quantum system when and if it appears in the natural world as an observable. In this sense, as the ψ function is a mix of mathematical and (undetermined) physical intentions, it can be characterized as a “mathema-physical object.” Closed in itself, it reveals in advance what can be found in a quantum system. However, the determinate properties it will exhibit are dependent on further mathematical and physical procedures, so that the quantum system the ψ function defines cannot, locked in itself, be accounted for as already having these definite properties (there is no possibility of a backward attribution), but only the potential of having them. Thus, London’s insight that the concept of objectivity must be reconsidered in light of quantum physics is quite understandable. The ψ function is a mathematical object of pure thought. Nevertheless, it fulfills objectivity conditions that allow it to become a physical object of natural science as it conveys an undetermined, blind intention of the physical that puts the whole set of its real possibilities as an “effective state.”

Still, in light of Husserl’s theory of the noema, it is plain what is still missing here: the reference to a determined object or, to say it in strict Husserlian terms, the X that is intentionally meant by the noema and eventually posited and given according to the conditions of rational belief. In a nutshell, what still lacks here is both the object in the how or in the manner it is intended, and the object simpliciter, which is intended. This is the path that leads to the quantum theory of the observables. It entails a huge amount of mathematical pure thinking, supported by a chain of mediated evidences and justifications guided by expectations about what the properties of a physical system are (spin, position, momentum, and so on): State vectors on a Hilbert space that can be infinite dimensional, the self-adjoint Hermitian operator (taking values in the set of real numbers) related to the physical quantity that will be measured, the eigenvector and the density operator (given by Born’s rule) for the expectation of eigenvalues corresponding to the eigenstates of the observable. At last, according to a phenomenological approach, with the eigenvector and the density operator, one has an object in the how, Obw, that nevertheless still misses a definite value, an eigenstate. Thus, here one has finally a definite objectual intention that is directed to something that can appear in the region nature. Still, taking an object in the how of its determinations but already ranging over a set of probabilities for possible eigenstates, it remains undetermined and in need of a final complementation by a fulfilling-determining act. This is the transcendental locus of the “observer.”

Indeed, the jumping to an eigenstate amounts to the Copenhagen theory of the “collapse” of the wave function and to London’s account of the observer. In our phenomenological approach, it is tantamount to reaching the object simpliciter which is intended. There is no need to return to technicalities that were dealt with before. The point is that, in a phenomenological framework, the mathema-physical noematic sense along with its several ways of evidential justification only intends a definite object in the region nature when (i) there is a characterization of what the objectual X is, as the “central noematic moment,”Footnote 58 (ii) there is an intention of the X in the how of its determinacies and (iii) the latter act enters with a perceptual act in a fulfilling and determining relationship,Footnote 59 so that (a) the set of probabilities meant by the object in the how is cut, (b) there is a doxastic perceptual giveness of an event, G = gk, which is interpreted in the framework of the noematic sense: F = fk.Footnote 60

Putting everything together, two concluding remarks are in order.

1. The measuring problem in quantum physics cannot have a transcendental-phenomenological definite answer. On closer inspection, it was not even Fritz London’s intention in his essay (with Bauer) and particularly in the eleventh section that was seemingly written by him alone. As a professional physicist and an incipient philosopher, he thought the phenomenological theory of objectifying acts and objectual constitution was able to account for the status of an observer as a subject of knowledge, especially regarding the process of constitution of new states-of-affairs from the mathematical calculus on the ψ function and the perceptual observation of the outcomes of its interaction with an apparatus. It was precisely this endeavor that I tried to develop, interpreting his remarks in the framework of Husserl’s concept of the noema and its relation to an intentional object and especially in light of a phenomenology of reason, i.e., of the constitution of reality. Concerning French’s idea that the phenomenological “birth” of an “I” (if there is one at all) will have something to do with the collapse of the wave function, avoiding through this move the mind-matter dualistic objections, would be tantamount to explaining the less obscure by the more obscure and to compromise phenomenology with an controversial interpretation—the so-called “Copenhagen interpretation”—where something like mysterious “collapses” appear as a postulate of the theory. In fact, neither the concept of a collapse is uncontroversial and universally accepted (there are other views, indeed) nor should phenomenology be called for substantiating such a mysterious hypothesis concerning the behavior of quantum physical systems. Despite London’s reliance on the concept of a collapse, his account does not, as I stressed, intend to put the conscious observer as its trigger, but only to show how knowledge grows by a series of objectifying acts that should not be thought of as physically creative of the phenomena observed, but as constitutive of new objectual sense-formations. At last, there remains a kind of circularity here: there is an observation when there is a collapse of the wave function and there is a collapse when an observation takes place—one thing is explained in terms of the other. London remains wholly inside this riddle, which is one of the oddities of the Copenhagen interpretation.

2. As I have already pointed out, when the objectual constitution is put together with the relevant regional ontologies, and when its positings are examined, one cannot avoid concluding that quantum theory conveys a poor ontology of nature and makes assertions that are not suited for an ontological interpretation at all. This is an old stigma linked to quantum physics: On the one hand, it is the most reliable theory we ever had, and, on the other hand, the most silent regarding what one can say about nature based on its formulae alone. This is evident when one looks at the status of its major theoretical piece, the wave function itself. Indeed, the wave function accounts for all quantum phenomena, but it is not the mathematical description of a phenomenon itself (as, for instance, the equations of mechanics or of electromagnetism are), i.e., of something to which a positing of being could be done. It has no positivity. Indeed, it is just a mathematical structure: A complex-valued probability amplitude, from which the probability of the measurements made on the system can be derived (but not determined when it comes to the single result) when a set of commuting operators is chosen. So, one has, on one side, a mathematical object that is successful in predicting the results of measurements, if they are done. As London stressed, it has the necessary conditions to being considered as a pure object of thought: it prescribes the type of mathematical operations and procedures that can be made on it. Nevertheless, on the other side, since the wave function is construed as the superposition of all possible states of a quantum system, a superposition that is thought of as a state on its own, what is really posited by it is the effectiveness of all possibilities of events, but no event at all, as if it was describing a world where nothing happens, a “paralyzed” or “frozen” world, a world that does not have the sense-content for being posited as such. Briefly, it does not contain the sufficient conditions to be posited as having actuality on its own. As a result of this strange situation, one must conclude that the properties a quantum system exhibits when proved by an apparatus cannot be projected backwards into the system before the measurement. This is indeed, as London remarks, a non-classical situation.

Nevertheless, if one is not realistic about the wave function (saying that it does not convey a positing of reality), the baffling statement that the properties a quantum system has when measured cannot describe what the very same quantum system is without measurement can be, if not overturned, at least accounted for in a different way. What if the properties exhibited by the quantum system were the result of its interaction with the apparatus? What if what comes to be measured are the ways a complex system interacts with another complex system? What if great part of the uncertainties concerning the evolution of a quantum system were based not on a non-classical feature of “ontological” probabilities, but in the incapacity of having a complete domain over the physical connections a quantum entity has with the apparatus with which it is interacting?

Aerts raised this hypothesis, which he called a “hidden-measurement” theory.Footnote 61 In this sense, quantum probabilities would be connected to a general theory of measurement. There are measurements that simply verify what the case is (for instance, looking at a distant star); there are measurements that disturb the system observed (almost all quantum measurements are of this kind); moreover, there are measurements that destroy what is measured (a beam of light waves passing through a vertical and then through a horizontal filter, i.e., crossed polarization); finally, there are measurements that create the very property that is observed, because it is a relational or an emergent property of the complex system formed by the quantum entity and the apparatus, instead of being a real property of each one alone. If one thinks of measurements in light not only of the perturbative, but also of the creative feature, then one soon realizes that the real properties of a system cannot be made equivalent to the calculus of the relational properties it will have when interacting with another system. However, this is the situation one faces with the wave function. It calculates the properties the quantum system will have when and if it interacts, and this set of potential properties is put as what the system actually is, in such a way that it does not coincide with one of them, but with the effectiveness of all its possibilities. In a nutshell, the wave function prevents an effective, sound, realistic account of the quantum system as such, telling us that what it is (before interaction) is no more than the set of potential relational properties it will possibly have (after interaction).Footnote 62 In so doing, the reification of the wave function prevents a positive description of what the structure of quantum entities is, so that they can exhibit such and such relational or emergent properties, as it was early suggested, for instance, by de Broglie pilot-wave theory and, to a certain extent, also by Bohm and others. This is not tantamount to saying that real properties must come firstly and only afterwards relational properties, as if the category of substance preceded that of relation. This only means that, if measurements are interactions that create new relational or emergent properties, one needs a positive account of what the interacting systems are after all. This is the ontological indigency of the Copenhagen “spirit” to which London also belongs, despite his brilliancy as a physicist and the relevance of his philosophical queries.

Can phenomenology help, as London seemingly expected?

Only at the cost of transforming itself and questioning again, with a fresh look, the issues that are truly at stake for a philosophy of nature.

In my opinion, a phenomenologically inspired approach to these new issues would firstly include the critique of the substruction of an exact nature as the objectual correlate of mathematical physics, developing some of Husserl’s last insights from a new perspective. Secondly, it would also include a regional ontology of nature as a morphological, non-exact realm, where Husserl’s mereology would be based on a more fundamental theory of interdependent parts in which the complete analysis principle (a whole is resoluble into an identifiable set of elements) and the summative principle (a whole is “nothing more” than the sum of its parts) no longer hold. Finally, an extremely important task for a full understanding of the position in which we are would be the back reference of the theoretical idealizations of physics to the physicist’s cultural and historical lifeworld.

However, these are matters that exceed the scope of this article and the ingenuity of his author.

They are also beyond Fritz London’s philosophical agenda.

As Bohr also belongs to the quantum “orthodoxy,” later (in the 1950’) named the “Copenhagen interpretation,” one must recognize that there were at least two tendencies and not a uniform, monolithic trend. Namely, von Neumann’s approach, and the complementarity-approach by Bohr, which never spoke of a “collapse” of the wave function and always insisted in physicalizing the observer, taking it as a physical apparatus submitted to a “classical-like” separation from the measured phenomena. As far as I can see, given his later works on the covalent bond from a quantum perspective, London is more in keeping with von Neumann’s construal.

  16. Bitbol (2000, 47) drew attention to the curious case of Darwin’s work in wave mechanics. Darwin’s fundamental issue regards the corpuscle-wave dualism that plagued quantum mechanics since its very beginning. The article tries to put together a wave-like physical, objective description of the quantum systems and a particle-like interpretation when they came to be observed. Rejecting the prevailing “philosophy of modern physics that our theories should only contain observable quantities,” i.e., a kind of scientific positivism professed by Heisenberg and so many others, his proposal amounts to consider, at an ontological basis, “the wave function ψ as the central feature of the quantum theory” (Darwin 1929, 391). The main idea is that the wave description is enough, and that the physicist can postpone indefinitely the account of the physical behavior of the system by the language of particles and trajectories, needing to introduce it only when, and if, a measurement is finally made. The measurement entails a gain of information concerning the system, and the particle-like representation is an interpretation introduced by the conscious observer. As he expresses himself in the final part of his essay, which is worth quoting at length, “[…] there is no need to invoke particle-like properties in the unobserved parts of any occurrence, since the wave function ψ will give all the necessary effects. […] The observed result is given by interpreting the effect of the original system […] If we are considering the scintillations of α-particles, we usually think of the stopping of the α-particle as the observation. The scintillation is due to the bursting of a small zinc sulphide crystal, and we might include the atoms of the crystal in our ψ along with the α-wave. We might go further by including the emitted radiation and take as the observation the intensity of light in the retina eye. Even this is not all, for we might include the photoelectrons emitted from the retina, and the optic nerve also, but somewhere in the brain we are absolutely compelled to stop. We can say that all occurrences right up the brain are non-committal, in that they contain no definite implication where and when the α-particle came; it is only after our consciousness has animated the proceedings that it is possible to infer back and describe what happened in the familiar language of particles. If these ideas are admissible, we can put the inexplicable feature of the quantum theory, the irreconcilability of wave and particle, in exactly the place where we have got in any case to have an inexplicability, in the transfer from objective to subjective.” (Darwin 1929, 392–393, my emphases).

  40. More recently (French 2020) stressed the supposed phenomenological framework of London’s reference to immanent knowledge in the act of observation. I disagree with his argument. Firstly, the text by Husserl he quotes, putting “pure immanence” as a principle of authentic knowledge (Ideas-I, section 59; Husserl 1983, 136) refers to the exclusion of material-eidetic disciplines by the epoché, and not to reflection. There, “pure immanence” means reflective description of the noetic-noematic structures, i.e. both the really (reel) and the intentionally immanent, and not to the consciousness of an “I.” Husserl himself noted in a handwritten note that the expression “pure immanence” could misleadingly “look as though only real data were to be taken into account.” Secondly, transcendental reflection entails: The epoché or exclusion of any transcendency; the discovery of the new realm of being of transcendental subjectivity, given to itself in transcendental experience; the isolation of an immutable point of identity, the “I,” which is empty and given as a transcendence in immanence. Clearly, this is not what is at stake when the physicist performs an act of observation. The “immanent knowledge” that is at stake is the psychological capability for self-awareness, to which there is, to be sure, a phenomenological description at a transcendental level. But describing phenomenologically an act of self-awareness and being aware, as a physical observer, of its own sates are very different things.

As far as I can see, French ingenious insight is that the “I” will spring from the quantum superposition, so that, by means of self-awareness of its own internal states, the "I" will be capable of bring the superposition to a definite state. However, I believe that this hermeneutic strategy does not work. One faces a dilemma: Either the “birth” of the “I” is the cause of the collapse of the wave function, and in this case London is committed to a mentalistic account that is open to Putnam’s criticisms, or the “I” does not collapse the wave function, and, in such a case, the superposition will remain, at the sub-"I" quantum level, while at the "I"-level there will be a definite subjective state that has no correspondence with the quantum objective state. It is worthless to “dematerialize” the “I” by means of a transcendental account. If it emerges from the superposition, one will have a naturalistic account for the genesis of the “I” and not a transcendental one, because, for the latter, the subjectivity is the prius, which cannot be derived from a non-subjective realm. “Consciousness arrives out of consciousness,” as Husserl once remarked, even from the depths of the unconscious. However, this phenomenological “unconscious,” if there is one, is itself for consciousness, as a process of self-constitution that cannot be equated with a naturalistic account concerning the way a conscious subject arrives from an “objective,” physical quantum superposition. For a Husserlian inspired phenomenology, the question is not how there is a subject for an objective world, but rather how there is an objective world for a subject. It is not a matter of deriving subjectivity by spontaneous acts of reflection from an objective world as described by quantum physics. For Husserl, this would be Naturalism and Objectivism, thus, the “forgetting” of subjectivity. The “sitting point” of Husserl’s subjectivity is not the quantum realm but the lifeworld. The quantum realm is rather a substruction performed on the subjective-relative structure of the lifeworld. For a phenomenological theory of physical science, the issue is the description of the sense institutions that constitute the world of physics as a true world “behind” the lifeworld.
