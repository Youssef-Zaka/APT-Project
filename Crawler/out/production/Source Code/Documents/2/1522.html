Abstract

Recently, Frauchiger and Renner proposed a Gedankenexperiment, which was claimed to be able to prove that quantum theory cannot consistently describe the use of itself. Here we show that the conclusions of Frauchiger and Renner actually came from their incorrect description of some quantum states. With the correct description there will be no inconsistent results, no matter which quantum interpretation theory is used. Especially, the Copenhagen interpretation can satisfy all the three assumptions (C), (Q), and (S) of Frauchiger and Renner simultaneously, thus it has no problem consistently describing the use of itself.

Introduction

In a recent publication of Frauchiger and Renner [1], a Gedankenexperiment was proposed, which was claimed to be able to lead to inconsistent conclusions with a self-referential use of quantum theory. Thus it seems to prove that quantum theory cannot consistently describe the use of itself. The Gedankenexperiment was highly praised to be “more involved than Schrödinger’s cat” [2, 3], and caught great interests immediately [4,5,6,7,8,9,10]. Here we show that the original conclusions of Frauchiger and Renner actually came from their own inconsistent use of quantum theories in the description of the quantum systems. With the correct description there will be no inconsistent results. Especially, the Copenhagen interpretation can satisfy all the three assumptions (C), (Q), and (S) of Frauchiger and Renner simultaneously, thus it has no problem consistently describing the use of itself. Unlike [4,5,6,7,8,9,10], our result reveals that there is no need to introduce new hypotheses in quantum theory or modify the original Gedankenexperiment.

The next section is a brief review on the Gedankenexperiment and the result of Frauchiger and Renner. Then in Sect. 3, we will elaborate what went wrong in Frauchiger and Renner original reasoning, and why the inconsistency will disappear when applying quantum interpretation theories correctly. In Sect. 4 we will address the lesson that we learned from this case. Finally, our conclusions will be summarized and compared with related works from other researchers in Sect. 5.

The Frauchiger–Renner Gedankenexperiment

In brief, Frauchiger and Renner proposed the following three assumptions in Boxes 2–4 of [1].


Assumption (Q)

Suppose that agent A has established that

      Statement\(A^{(i)}\): “System S is in state \(\left| \psi \right\rangle _{S}\) at time \(t_{0}\) .”

Suppose furthermore that agent A knows that

      Statement\(A^{(ii)}\): “The value x is obtained by a measurement of S w.r.t. the family \(\{\pi _{x}^{t_{0}}\}_{x\in \chi }\) of Heisenberg operators relative to time \(t_{0}\), which is completed at time t.”

If \(\left\langle \psi \right| \pi _{\xi }^{t_{0}}\left| \psi \right\rangle =1\) for some \(\xi \in \chi\) then agent A can conclude that

      Statement\(A^{(iii)}\): “I am certain that \(x=\xi\) at time t.”


Assumption (C)

Suppose that agent A has established that

      Statement\(A^{(i)}\): “I am certain that agent \(A^{\prime }\), upon reasoning within the same theory as the one I am using, is certain that \(x=\xi\) at time t.”

Then agent A can conclude that

      Statement\(A^{(ii)}\): “I am certain that \(x=\xi\) at time t.”


Assumption (S)

Suppose that agent A has established that

      Statement\(A^{(i)}\): “I am certain that \(x=\xi\) at time t.”

Then agent A must necessarily deny that

      Statement\(A^{(ii)}\): “I am certain that \(x\ne \xi\) at time t.”


Then the main conclusion of [1] is the following no-go theorem.

Theorem 1

Any theory that satisfies assumptions (Q), (C), and (S) yields contradictory statements when applied to the Gedankenexperiment of Box 1 (of [1] ).

This Gedankenexperiment is a procedure among four agents \({\bar{F}}\), F, \({\bar{W}}\) and W. They repeat the following steps in rounds \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\) until the halting condition in the last step is satisfied:

At time n : 00, Agent \({\bar{F}}\) measures a quantum system R in state

$$\begin{aligned} \left| init\right\rangle _{R}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}+\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| tails\right\rangle _{R} \end{aligned}$$
(1)

and denote the outputs as \(r=heads\) or \(r=tails\). She sets the spin S of a particle to \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) if \(r=heads\), and to \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\equiv \sqrt{1/2}(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}+\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S})\) if \(r=tails\), and sends it to F.

At n : 10, Agent F measures S in the basis \(\{\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S},\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\}\), recording the outcome \(z\in \{-1/2,+1/2\}\).

At n : 20, Agent \({\bar{W}}\) measures lab \({\bar{L}}\) (containing system R and agent \({\bar{F}}\)) in a basis containing

$$\begin{aligned} \left| {\overline{ok}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}-\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{ {\bar{L}}}\right) . \end{aligned}$$
(2)

Here \(\left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\) and \(\left| {\bar{t}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\) are defined as the states of lab \({\bar{L}}\) at the end of the first step, depending on whether \(r=heads\) or \(r=tails\), respectively. If the outcome associated to \(\left| {\overline{ok}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\) occurs he announces \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) and else \({\bar{w}}={\overline{fail}}\).

At n : 30, Agent W measures lab L (containing system S and agent F) in a basis containing

$$\begin{aligned} \left| ok\right\rangle _{L}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| -\frac{1 }{2}\right\rangle _{L}-\left| +\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}\right) . \end{aligned}$$
(3)

Here \(\left| -1/2\right\rangle _{L}\) and \(\left| +1/2\right\rangle _{L}\) are defined as the state of lab L depending on whether the incoming spin was \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) or \(\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\), respectively. If the outcome associated to \(\left| ok\right\rangle _{L}\) occurs he announces \(w=ok\) and else \(w=fail\).

At n : 40, if \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) and \(w=ok\) then the experiment is halted.

According to [1], this Gedankenexperiment can make the agents arrive at conflicting conclusions (thus violating Assumption (C)) when they all employ the same form of quantum theory. Some main points of the reasoning is briefly reviewed below.

(i) From agent \({\bar{F}}\)’s point of view:

Suppose that \({\bar{F}}\) got \(r=tails\) in round n. Then according to the above experimental instructions, she can make the statement

Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:01}\): “The spin S is in state \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) at time n : 10 .”

Agent \({\bar{F}}\) could then conclude that the later state of lab L is

$$\begin{aligned} U_{S\rightarrow L}^{10\rightarrow 20}\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}+\left| +\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}\right) \end{aligned}$$
(4)

(as presented in the paragraph before Eq. (4) of [1]), which is orthogonal to \(\left| ok\right\rangle _{L}\). Thus \({\bar{F}}\) will further infer that

Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\): “I am certain that W will observe \(w=fail\) at time n : 31.”

(ii) From agent F’s point of view:

Suppose that F observed \(z=+1/2\) in this round. Note that if \({\bar{F}}\) got \(r=heads\) at time n : 01, she should have sent F the state \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\), which can never be observed as \(z=+1/2\). Therefore, F can conclude that

Statement\(F^{n:12}\): “I am certain that \({\bar{F}}\) knows that \(r=tails\) at time n : 01.”

Then F can further infer from Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\) that

Statement\(F^{n:13}\): “I am certain that \({\bar{F}}\) is certain that W will observe \(w=fail\) at time n : 31.”

This is because F infers that when \({\bar{F}}\) got \(r=tails\), the state of lab L is Eq. (4) which can never lead to the outcome \(w=ok\).

(iii) From agent \({\bar{W}}\)’s point of view:

Agent \({\bar{W}}\) can be certain that there will always be \(({\bar{w}},z)\ne ( {\overline{ok}},-1/2)\), i.e., the outcomes \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) and \(z=-1/2\) can never occur simultaneously. This is because, according to [1], the state of \({\bar{L}}\otimes S\) at time n : 10 is

$$\begin{aligned} U_{R\rightarrow {\bar{L}}S}^{00\rightarrow 10}\left| init\right\rangle _{R}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}+\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| {\bar{t}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S} \end{aligned}$$
(5)

(as presented in the paragraph before Eq. (6) of [1]), which is orthogonal to \(\left| {\overline{ok}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\). Consequently, in any round once \({\bar{W}}\) obtains \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\), he can be sure that

Statement\({\bar{W}}^{n:22}\): “I am certain that F knows that \(z=+1/2\) at time n : 11.”

Combining with the reasoning in the above point (ii), he can infer from Statements\(F^{n:12}\), \({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\) and \(F^{n:13}\) that

Statement\({\bar{W}}^{n:23}\): “I am certain that F is certain that W will observe \(w=fail\) at time n : 31.”

Putting points (i), (ii) and (iii) together, we see that once \({\bar{W}}\) obtains \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\), there should always be \(w=fail\). Thus the halting condition (i.e., both \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) and \(w=ok\) occur) in the last step of the Gedankenexperiment can never be satisfied.

But according to Eq. (7) of [1], \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) and \(w=ok\) can occur in the same round with probability 1/12. Therefore, when this case indeed occurs, a contradiction is reached. (See page 4 and Table 3 of [1] for full details.)

Our Result

In our opinion, however, the above contradiction (i.e., the violation of Assumption (C)) actually comes from the mistaken reasoning in [1], not from quantum theory itself. There are two different ways to see where the mistake lies, depending on which quantum interpretation theory is used.

(1) In quantum interpretation theories which believe that wavefunctions collapse when being measured.

A prominent example is the Copenhagen interpretation. While it has many variations, according to one widely accepted version, “central to the popular image of the Copenhagen interpretation is the idea that observation-induced wave packet collapse is a mode of dynamical evolution unique to measurement interactions” (as stated at the beginning of Sects. 2 of [11], see also [12,13,14]). From this point of view, the above Eqs. (4) and (5) that Ref. [1] introduced (as appeared in the paragraphs before Eqs. (4) and (6) of [1], respectively) are not the correct description of the corresponding states.

To make it more obvious, let us write the initial state of quantum system R that \({\bar{F}}\) will measure at time n : 00 as

$$\begin{aligned} \left| init\right\rangle _{R}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}+e^{i\theta }\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}. \end{aligned}$$
(6)

When taking the phase difference \(\theta =0\) it is exactly the same as Eq. ( 1) that appeared in the Gedankenexperiment in [1]. From the Copenhagen interpretation point of view, “every act of observation is by its very nature an irreversible process”, “the correlations between ... different states disappear when the state of equilibrium is reached; they are wiped out ... in the process of recording the result of the measurement” (see the 2nd paragraph of page 7 of [15]). Therefore, after \({\bar{F}}\) measures \(\left| init\right\rangle _{R}\) at time n : 00 in the basis \(\{\left| heads\right\rangle _{R},\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\}\), the Copenhagen interpretation implies that \(\left| init\right\rangle _{R}\) collapses to either \(\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}\) or \(\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\)irreversibly. Either way, the information on \(\theta\) is lost. Especially, even there was \(\theta \ne 0\) at the beginning, after the measurement the resultant state is still \(\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}\) or \(\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\), which shows no difference from the \(\theta =0\) case. Thus, recovering the original value of \(\theta\) from the resultant state is impossible. Consequently, there is no reason to believe that the later states of \({\bar{L}} \otimes S\) should take the form \(U_{R\rightarrow {\bar{L}}S}^{00\rightarrow 10}\left| init\right\rangle _{R}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| {\bar{h}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}+e^{i\theta }\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}} }\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) with \(\theta\) preserved in its original value, because this is a reversible unitary evolvement of \({\bar{L}}\otimes S\), not a measurement in the sense of the Copenhagen interpretation. Instead, after the measurement there should no longer be any fixed phase difference between \(\left| {\bar{h}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) and \(\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\).

Therefore, after \({\bar{F}}\) measured system R, from the outcome she herself knows that \({\bar{L}}\otimes S\) will be in a pure state \(\left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) or \(\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) with probability 1/3 or 2/3, respectively. But from agent \({\bar{W}}\)’s point of view, as he has no information on \({\bar{F}}\)’s measurement outcome before he makes his own measurement, he does not know the purification of the state of \({\bar{L}} \otimes S\). Then the correct description of the state of \({\bar{L}}\otimes S\) is no longer Eq. (5) (which is a pure state with a fixed phase difference \(\theta =0\)). Instead, \({\bar{L}}\otimes S\) will appear to \({\bar{W}}\) as a mixture (which has no fixed phase difference between its components) described by the density matrix

$$\begin{aligned} \rho _{{\bar{L}}S}^{10}= & {} \frac{1}{3}\left( \left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{ {\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\right) \left( \left\langle \downarrow \right| _{S}\otimes \left\langle {\bar{h}} \right| _{{\bar{L}}}\right) \nonumber \\&+\frac{2}{3}\left( \left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\right) \left( \left\langle \rightarrow \right| _{S}\otimes \left\langle {\bar{t}}\right| _{{\bar{L}} }\right) . \end{aligned}$$
(7)

For the same reason, after F measured \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) in the basis \(\{\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S},\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\}\) at time n : 10, the wavefunction collapsed from \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) to either \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) or \(\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\), and the information on the phase difference between \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) and \(\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\) in \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}=\sqrt{1/2} (\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}+\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S})\) is lost. Thus, from agent W’s point of view, the correct description of the states of lab L is no longer the pure state shown in Eq. (4). Instead, lab L will be in a mixture described by the density matrix

$$\begin{aligned} \rho _{L}^{20}=\frac{1}{2}\left( \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}\left\langle -\frac{1}{2}\right| +\left| +\frac{1}{2} \right\rangle _{L}\left\langle +\frac{1}{2}\right| \right) . \end{aligned}$$
(8)

With these correct descriptions, we can see that unlike Eq. (4), now the state described by Eq. (8) is not orthogonal to \(\left| ok\right\rangle _{L}\) any more. Thus \({\bar{F}}\) cannot infer that W will necessarily observe \(w=fail\) at time n : 31 as she did in the above point (i). That is, the above Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\) no longer holds, which also breaks down the logical link between Statements\(F^{n:12}\) and \(F^{n:13}\).

Similarly, unlike Eq. (5), now the state described by Eq. (7 ) is not orthogonal to \(\left| {\overline{ok}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}} }\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\). Then \({\bar{W}}\) can no longer be certain that there will always be \(({\bar{w}},z)\ne ({\overline{ok}} ,-1/2)\) as he did in the above point (iii). Instead, when \({\bar{w}}=\overline{ ok}\) occurs, both the outcomes \(z=+1/2\) and \(z=-1/2\) are possible. That is, knowing \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) can no longer assure \({\bar{W}}\) that there must be \(z=+1/2\). Consequently, the Statement\({\bar{W}}^{n:22}\) does not hold, so that Statement\({\bar{W}}^{n:23}\) cannot be inferred either.

Thus we can see that the reasoning in page 4 of [1] is no longer valid, so that the statements in the last two columns of Table 3 of [1] cannot be inferred either.

In brief, if we take the point of view that measurements will make wavefunctions collapse irreversibly so that the information on the phase difference is erased, then the key reason why the Gedankenexperiment in [1] had led to the inconsistent result is that the agents in the Gedankenexperiment mistakenly described the states of the labs as pure states (i.e., Eqs. (4) and (5)). But in fact they should be mixtures (Eqs. (8) and (7)). Therefore, when using Copenhagen interpretation and similar quantum interpretations, quantum theory will not cause any inconsistent in this Gedankenexperiment so that Assumption (C) of [1] will not be violated.

It is also worth noting that some versions of the Copenhagen interpretation does not even allow the Frauchiger–Renner Gedankenexperiment in the first place. This is because these theories suggested that a quantum state belongs always to a microscopic object in connection with a measurement apparatus, and the latter should be described by classical physics, while the Frauchiger–Renner Gedankenexperiment treated it the other way. Nevertheless, even if we do not take this point of view, our above analysis showed that the collapse feature alone is already sufficient for disproving Frauchiger–Renner’s result.

(2) In quantum interpretation theories where there is no collapse of wavefunctions.

Some quantum interpretation theories (e.g., the many-worlds interpretation) exclude the idea of collapse of wavefunctions. Even some versions of the Copenhagen interpretation take this viewpoint too. In this case Eq. (5) can be taken as the correct description. This is because agent \(\bar{ W}\) can consider \({\bar{F}}\) and her particles as a unitarily-interacting closed system, and thus conclude that the post-measurement status is a coherent superposition. More detailedly, this is done as follows. Let \(\left| init\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| init\right\rangle _{S}\) denote the initial state of agent \({\bar{F}}\) and particle S right before she measures system R at time n : 00, and \(\left| {\bar{h}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) (or \(\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\)) denote the final state that \({\bar{F}}\) obtains the output \(r=heads\) (or \(r=tails\)) and sets the spin of S to \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) (or \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\)). From \({\bar{W}}\)’s point of view, instead of applying the projective operators \(\{\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}\left\langle heads\right| ,\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\left\langle tails\right| \}\) on system R (whose state is in Eq. (1)) alone, at time n : 00 lab \({\bar{L}}=R\otimes {\bar{F}}\) and particle S actually went through a unitary transformation

$$\begin{aligned} U_{{\bar{L}}S}^{00\rightarrow 10}\equiv \left| heads\right\rangle _{R}\left\langle heads\right| \otimes U_{{\bar{F}}S}^{{\bar{h}}}+\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\left\langle tails\right| \otimes U_{{\bar{F}}S}^{ {\bar{t}}}, \end{aligned}$$
(9)

where \(U_{{\bar{F}}S}^{{\bar{h}}}\) (or \(U_{{\bar{F}}S}^{{\bar{t}}}\)) is a unitary transformation on \({\bar{F}}\otimes S\) that maps \(\left| init\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| init\right\rangle _{S}\) into \(\left| \bar{h }\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) (or \(\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\)). Then the resultant state of \({\bar{L}} \otimes S\) is

$$\begin{aligned} \left| 10\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}S}\equiv & \;{} U_{{\bar{L}}S}^{00\rightarrow 10}(\left| init\right\rangle _{R}\otimes \left| init\right\rangle _{ {\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| init\right\rangle _{S}) \nonumber \\= &\; {} \sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}\otimes \left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S} \nonumber \\&+\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\otimes \left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}\otimes \left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}. \end{aligned}$$
(10)

Denoting

$$\begin{aligned} \left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}=\left| heads\right\rangle _{R}\otimes \left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}} \end{aligned}$$
(11)

and

$$\begin{aligned} \left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}=\left| tails\right\rangle _{R}\otimes \left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{F}}}, \end{aligned}$$
(12)

we successfully obtain Eq. (5) from Eq. (10).

From this deduction process, it can be seen that if we take Eq. (5) as valid, we actually accepted the following logic:

For any agent (such as \({\bar{W}}\) ) who knows nothing about what happened inside the lab (e.g., which outcome \({\bar{F}}\) had obtained), the state of the lab should be described as a quantum superposition, in which all terms corresponding to all possible outcomes inside the lab are included.

But in this scenario, another problem will arise in the original reasoning in [1]. As stressed in [1], all agents employ the same theory throughout the Gedankenexperiment. Therefore, when agent F measures S at time n : 10, from other agents’ point of view, lab \(L=S\otimes F\) should also be considered as a unitarily-interacting system. Let \(\left| init\right\rangle _{F}\) denote the initial state of agent F. Then the unitary transformation corresponding to this process can be expressed as

$$\begin{aligned} U_{L}^{10\rightarrow 20}\equiv \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\left\langle \downarrow \right| \otimes U_{F}^{(-)}+\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\left\langle \uparrow \right| \otimes U_{F}^{(+)}, \end{aligned}$$
(13)

where \(U_{F}^{(-)}\) (or \(U_{F}^{(+)}\)) is a unitary transformation on F that maps \(\left| init\right\rangle _{F}\) into \(\left| -1/2\right\rangle _{F}\) (or \(\left| +1/2\right\rangle _{F}\)). Here \(\left| -1/2\right\rangle _{F}\) (or \(\left| +1/2\right\rangle _{F}\)) denotes the state of F when the state of lab L is \(\left| -1/2\right\rangle _{L}\) (or \(\left| +1/2\right\rangle _{L}\)), i.e.,

$$\begin{aligned} \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}=\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\otimes \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{F} \end{aligned}$$
(14)

and

$$\begin{aligned} \left| +\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}=\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\otimes \left| +\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{F}. \end{aligned}$$
(15)

Applying Eq. (13) on Eq. (10) (i.e., Eq. (5)), we know that the resultant state of \({\bar{L}}\otimes L={\bar{L}}\otimes S\otimes F\) is

$$\begin{aligned}&I_{{\bar{L}}}\otimes U_{L}^{10\rightarrow 20}(\left| 10\right\rangle _{ {\bar{L}}S}\otimes \left| init\right\rangle _{F}) \nonumber \\&\quad =\sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\otimes \left| -\frac{1}{2} \right\rangle _{F} \nonumber \\&\qquad +\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \sqrt{ \frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\otimes \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{F}+\left| \uparrow \right\rangle _{S}\otimes \left| +\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{F}\right) \nonumber \\&\quad =\sqrt{\frac{1}{3}}\left| {\bar{h}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L} \nonumber \\&\qquad +\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\otimes \sqrt{ \frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}+\left| + \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}\right) \nonumber \\&\quad =\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| {\bar{h}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}+\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\right) \otimes \left( \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}+\frac{1}{2} \left| +\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}\right) \nonumber \\&\qquad +\sqrt{\frac{1}{12}}\left| {\overline{ok}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}} }\otimes \left( \left| ok\right\rangle _{L}-\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\left( \left| -\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle _{L}+\left| +\frac{1}{2} \right\rangle _{L}\right) \right) , \end{aligned}$$
(16)

where \(I_{{\bar{L}}}\) is the identity operator on lab \({\bar{L}}\). This equation shows that the outcome \(({\bar{w}},w)=({\overline{ok}},ok)\) can occur with probability 1/12, which is in agreement with Eq. (7) of [1] . But the important point is: since all agents employ the same theory, not only W, but also \({\bar{F}}\) and \({\bar{W}}\) are fully aware of this equation too. Note that Eq. (16) contains not only the term \(\sqrt{ 1/2}\left( \left| -1/2\right\rangle _{L}+\left| +1/2\right\rangle _{L}\right)\), but also other terms such as \(\left| ok\right\rangle _{L}\) . Thus it brings forth the following question: when W is going to measure lab L at time n : 30, which one is the correct description of the state of L, Eq. (16) or Eq. (4)?

To answer the question, we must note that different agents will have different point of view. Remind that \({\bar{W}}\) and W both take the perspective that no collapse occurs in measurements (otherwise our above point (1) applies), and they do not know whether \({\bar{F}}\) got \(r=tails\) or not before performing their own measurements. Therefore, following the same logic stated above that made Eq. (5) valid, from \({\bar{W}}\)’s and W’s point of view, all terms corresponding to both \(\left| {\bar{h}} \right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\) and \(\left| {\bar{t}}\right\rangle _{{\bar{L}}}\) should remain. That is, \({\bar{W}}\) and W will take Eq. (16) as correct, so they will not be surprised to find that the outcome \({\bar{w}}= {\overline{ok}}\) and \(w=ok\) can occur in the same round.

But from \({\bar{F}}\)’s point of view, she knows that she got \(r=tails\). Thus it seems that she herself could take Eq. (4) as the correct description. However, when she is to infer the result of other agents, it will be a different story. As she knows that “W does not know whether I got \(r=tails\)or not” and they all use the same quantum theory, if she is wise enough, she should be able to infer that “Wwill take Eq. (16) instead of Eq. (4) as the correct description of the state ofLbefore performing his measurement”. As a result, \({\bar{F}}\) should no longer make Statement\({\bar{F}} ^{n:02}\) that “I am certain that W will observe \(w=fail\) at time n : 31.”. Instead, she should infer from Eq. (16) that

Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02*}\): “I am certain that when \({\bar{W}}\) find \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\) at time n : 21, W will have a nonzero probability to observe \(w=ok\) at time n : 31 .”

Consequently, all those statements (especially Statements\(F^{n:13}\) and \({\bar{W}}^{n:23}\)) in Table 3 of [1] that were inferred from the old Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\) will no longer hold. Also, when agent \({\bar{W}}\) find \({\bar{w}}={\overline{ok}}\), he can make his own judgement from Eq. (16) directly that

Statement\({\bar{W}}^{n:23*}\): “I am certain that W will have a nonzero probability to observe \(w=ok\) at time n : 31 .”

Therefore, the inconsistent results will no longer exist.

In other words, if \({\bar{F}}\) insists that both Eqs. (4) and (5) are the correct description of the states of the corresponding systems even from W’s point of view, then she is in fact not using the same quantum theory consistently throughout the whole Gedankenexperiment. That is, upon writing the state of \({\bar{L}}\otimes S\) as Eq. (5), she is taking the perspective that no collapse of wavefunctions occurs in measurement, so that both the terms corresponding to \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) and \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) exist. But when she think that Eq. (4) is the correct description of the state of lab L even from W’s point of view (so that she can infer Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\)), she actually take collapse into her picture because only the term corresponding to \(\left| \rightarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) remains in Eq. (4) while the term corresponding to \(\left| \downarrow \right\rangle _{S}\) disappeared. This is how the inconsistent results were introduced.

The Role of Interpretation Theories

Taking points (1) and (2) in the previous section together, we see that no matter we take an interpretation theory with or without collapse, the contradiction result in the Frauchiger–Renner Gedanken experiment will always be avoided.

But this situation by no means indicates that interpretation theory is not important to quantum mechanics. On the contrary, it highlights the need for a researcher to make a specific choice on the interpretation theory, and use it consistently throughout the analysis of quantum phenomena. Otherwise, if we apply the quantum mechanical formalism without having a clear picture on the interpretation, there will be the chance to ask an improper question, which bonds to have no correct answer.

In the current case, we see the importance to always have in mind a fixed picture of what happens during quantum measurement, even though it does not matter whether this picture allows collapse or not. In some interpretation theories (e.g., Everett’s many-worlds interpretation), the quantum mechanical formalism comprises states and unitary evolution only [16]. The Born rule is not included as an axiom. Instead, it is taken as a part of the interpretation and has to be derived independently, so that the very meaning of the phrase “outcome of a measurement” can be defined. In fact, Born’s original idea was to apply a quantum state only to ensembles. But Assumption (Q) of [1] seems to have made the tacit premise that an individual system is associated with a certain quantum state. Even so, if we stick to a single interpretation, either with or without collapse, and apply it consistently to analysis the statements of the agents, then there will be no problem, as it was shown in the previous section. On the contrary, Ref. [1] made an inconsistent logic that Eq. (5) was derived without taking collapse into consideration, while Statement\({\bar{F}} ^{n:02}\) seems to miss the fact that if quantum states always evolve unitarily without collapse, then the outcome of w should remain in a superposition instead of taking one of the value with certainty, and Eq. (4) is actually half-way between the collapse picture (where it should be replaced by Eq. (8)) and the non-collapse picture (where it should be replaced by Eq.(16)). That is, the shifting use between collapse and non-collapse interpretations in [1] is the cause that eventually led to the inconsistency among Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\), Eqs. (4) and ( 5).

Conclusions and Comparison with Related Works

In summary, none of the quantum interpretation theories will violate Assumption (C) when being applied correctly. On one hand, if we take the perspective of point (1) in Sect. 3 that wavefunctions will collapse when being measured, then Eqs. (4) and (5) are incorrect and should be replaced by Eqs. (8) and (7). On the other hand, if we take the perspective of point (2) that all measurements can be phrased as a unitarily-interacting process without involving collapse of wavefunctions, then Eq. (5) could remain valid but Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\) becomes incorrect. Either way, it leads to Conclusion 1 of our current paper:

The inconsistent results in [1] come from its own faulty reasoning, and not from quantum theory itself. The fault is that they had not applied a single specific interpretation theory consistently throughout the analysis of their Gedankenexperiment.

Moreover, Table 4 of [1] already showed that the Copenhagen interpretation can satisfy both assumptions (Q) and (S). Therefore, combining with our result, we reach Conclusion 2:

The Copenhagen interpretation can fulfil all the three assumptions (C), (Q), and (S) simultaneously without causing contradictory statements, thus disprove Theorem 1 of [1].

Around the same time we completed the initial version of the current paper [17], we came to aware that other researchers also proposed different solutions to the inconsistency. Table 1 listed the main claims of these works (showed in chronological order).

From the table we can see that our result has a significant difference from these of Refs. [4, 6,7,8, 10]. That is, to avoid the inconsistency in the Gedankenexperiment, they all suggested to introduce additional hypothese or approach to quantum theory, while our conclusion 1 showed that there is no such need. Refs. [5, 9] made no modification to quantum theory either. But Ref. [5] actually modified the Gedankenexperiment, so that it is not dealing with the original problem in [1]. In Ref. [9] the author stated that “I find it hard to see how this assumption (assumption (C)) is violated in any interpretation of quantum mechanics, except possibly QBism”. But it has not pointed out rigorously which equations or statements of [1] went wrong when not using the Consistent Histories interpretation, and what is the key reason that led to this mistake. On the contrary, we revealed that all interpretation theories can satisfy Assumption (C) without modification, and Ref. [1] thought it cannot be satisfied simply because they themselves had used the interpretation theories inconsistently, so that they mistakenly believed that Statement\({\bar{F}}^{n:02}\), Eqs. (4) and (5) can be valid simultaneously.
