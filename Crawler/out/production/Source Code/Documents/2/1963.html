'Some competitive races': Runoffs abound for Orleans Parish School Board seats

  • BY DELLA HASSELLE | Staff writer

With only two candidates elected outright to the Orleans Parish School Board during Tuesday's election, and five races headed to a Dec. 5 runoff, experts agree there are too many missing pieces to analyze the next board's makeup or its influence on future school district policy.

But while the full image remains fuzzy, one thing is clear: in the first election to be held since state-run charter schools in New Orleans were brought back under local control in 2018, a landslide of political newcomers drew enough support and funding to seriously challenge the five incumbents who sought to retain their posts, marking a distinct departure from School Board elections in years past.

John Brown Sr., who has held his District 1 seat since 2015, was the lone incumbent re-elected. Ethan Ashley, Leslie Ellison and Nolan Marshall were pushed into runoffs for their 2nd, 4th and 7th District seats. Grisela Jackson, who had been appointed earlier this year to replace Ken Kleban in the 5th District, was edged out of a runoff by two other candidates.

District 1, with Brown, and District 3, where newcomer Olin Parker was elected, were the only two races to draw just two candidates.

Educator Carlos Zervigon and college coach Erica Martinez are headed for a runoff in District 6, which did not have an incumbent.

"The good news is we have had some competitive races and incredible turnout," said Caroline Roemer, executive director of the Louisiana Association of Public Charter Schools. "And what's going on in Louisiana and New Orleans as a whole, is people are talking about education. Polling shows even nationally that education ranked in the top four issues people brought up this election."

Incumbent John Brown, newcomer Olin Parker elected to Orleans Parish School Board; 5 races headed to runoff

The new wave of civic engagement can be at least in part credited to the United Teachers of New Orleans, according to special education instructor and union organizer Lauren Jewett, who said that work done to encourage candidates, particularly teachers, began years ago.

"In order to ensure democracy for parents, teachers, and community members, we wanted to ensure a race in every district," Jewett said. "Because when you don't have candidates in every district, it doesnt bring to light issues and concerns happening in every school."

Depending on what voters decide next month, the work could pay off for the union, an analysis shows.

Both board members elected Tuesday were endorsed by UTNO, as were three of the other five candidates still in the race: teacher Chanel Payne, running against Ashley; Xavier student Antoinette Williams, running against former City Council aide Katherine Baudouinin the 5th District race; and Marshall, running against parent union member Kayonna Armstrong.

Most incumbents vowed to continue work already put in motion. Marshall, for instance, wants to focus on more districtwide services that help all charter students. Ellison has vowed to find ways to expand existing, high-performing charter networks. And Ashley is focused on finding more ways to ensure all students continue to have access to technology and adequate internet.

Some of the candidates challenging them, in the meantime, say they're not afraid to challenge status quo, including those who are union-backed.

"We hope the School Board will be more open and listening to concerns of teachers and parents and community in way that hasn't looked so in the past," Jewett said.

Payne, for instance, prefers true community schools to OneApp, which places children in schools based on parent preference and asystem algorithm, and is designed to be equitable but has garnered criticism.Armstrong, who is not UTNO-backed, hopes to upend a charter school model that's been in place the past 15 years.

And some candidates have said they're in favor of collective bargaining for teachers, which hasn't happened in earnest on a district-wide level since charters began to proliferate after 2005's Hurricane Katrina. Romero is among those who have criticized Superintendent Henderson Lewis Jr.'s role as charter authorizer and have called for a closer evaluation of the superintendent's duties.

Baudouin, who is not union-backed, wants to recruit new charter school leaders and board members to work closely with the School Board, while Zervigon wants a better education alternative than virtual learning and Martinez wants new teacher mentoring programs.

Some stakeholders, including Patrick Dobard, the CEO of the nonprofit New Schools for New Orleans, have also pointed out that there's unity among candidates, too, and that most agree about important issues surrounding equity, funding and more.

"We have turned a corner in New Orleans where we're not hung up on issues around unions and charters, but big system-wide policy challenges that can be solved for better outcomes for kids," Dobard said.

For instance, most candidates said they wanted students of color to have more access to A-rated schools and that they wanted to see the local district expand mental health services so schools with smaller budgets get adequate support for students.

With an eye on future budget cuts due to the COVID-19 pandemic, and the impact of a decline in oil and gas and tourism-based tax dollars, some candidates also said they were looking to leverage political relationships, seek grant dollars and eliminate duplicative spending to bolster the district's budget.

Teacher pay and retention has been another talking point, along with the expansion of education offerings in schools, such as more STEM programs.

Many of those talking points also aligned with a platform publicized by Forward New Orleans for Public Schools. The coalition doesn't include local teacher or parent unions but does partner with NSNO and some organizations that have funneled funds into School Board races, like Education Reform Now.

And all stakeholders, including Roemer, said they were pleased to see the level of engagement and focus on making public education better.

"Whether we agree with each other on how to get at solving problems, Im going to celebrate that were talking about it," Roemer said. "We're generating candidates and generating contributions."
