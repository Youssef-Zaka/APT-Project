Pronunciation: 
Brit. Hear pronunciation/ˈmakəwɛl/
, 
U.S. Hear pronunciation/ˈmækəˌwɛl/
, 
Caribbean English /ˈmakawɛl/
Forms:  1800s macaurel, 1800s mackawel, 1800s mahajuel, 1800s– macajuel, 1900s– macaquel, 1900s– machauel. (Show Less)
Origin: A borrowing from Spanish. Etymon: Spanish macaurel.
Etymology: < South American Spanish macaurel kind of venomous snake (J. Gumilla 1741), kind of boa (A. Codazzi 1841, or earlier) < a South American Indian language of Venezuela.
In the early 19th cent. the English word (in the form macaurel) is also occasionally used to denote a venomous snake; compare the following, ultimately based on J. Gumilla El Orinoco ilustrado y defendido (1741):
1802   R. Kerr tr. J. Gumilla in tr. Comte de Buffon Nat. Hist. Oviparous Quadrupeds & Serpents IV. 318   Its [sc. the coral serpent's] venom is equally virulent in all, and there is no serpent, except the macaurel [Fr. la Couleuvre Macaurel, Sp. las culebras macaureles], whose bite is more dangerous.
(Show Less)
Caribbean (chiefly Trinidad, Tobago, and Guyana).
Thesaurus »
Categories »
 

  A boa constrictor.

1838   E. L. Joseph Warner Arundell III. vi. 90   I found that an enormous mackawel, or boa-constrictor, of about twenty-five feet in length, had caught the poor beast in its fold.
1858   L. A. A. de Verteuil Trinidad 127   The Macajuel, whenever irritated, inflates its body, and then loudly emits a foetid and sickening breath, which produces a sensation of faintness.
1871   Weekly Times (Melbourne) 15 Apr. 3/3   The true boa, which is here called the Macajuel, is striped as well as spotted.
1949   Daily Gleaner (Kingston) 2 Dec. 9/4   With an abundance of macajuel and bushmasters in the forest, businessmen now see the possibility of making leather from snake skins for export to the United States.
2014   M. Williams New Beginning (e-book, accessed 9 July 2020) 138   He was certain that a huge snake—the feared reptile in the forest—a macajuel, was approaching and that very shortly he would be swallowed.
