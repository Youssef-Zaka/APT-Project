Irish English /ɡrɒː/
Forms:  1800s gra', 1800s– gra, 1800s– gradh, 1800s– gragh (rare and nonstandard), 1800s– grah (now rare), 1800s– grá, 1800s– grádh, 1900s gráh, 1900s– graw. (Show Less)
Origin: A borrowing from Irish. Etymon: Irish grá.
Etymology: < Irish grá, †grádh love (Early Irish grád), further etymology unknown.
With use as a term of endearment compare the Irish vocative a ghrá  , †a ghrádh   (see agra n.).

Irish English.
Categories »
 

†1. As a term of endearment: ‘my love’; ‘dear’. Cf. agra n. Obsolete.

1707   G. Farquhar Beaux Stratagem iv. 46   I am your Priest, Gra; and your Conscience is under my Hands.
1757   T. Smollett Reprisal ii. x. 42   And now I'll go and put the young lady below water, where she may laugh in her own sleeve, gra.
1771   T. Smollett Humphry Clinker I. 55   Now, I believe you are my friend sure enough, gra.
1838   S. C. Hall Light & Shadows Irish Life II. 69   ‘Bonneen gra,’ she continued, addressing the animal, ‘Bonneen gra, how's every bit o' ye? Bonneen was a heart's joy, a cushla!’

1707—1838(Hide quotations)

 
Thesaurus »
Categories »
 

 2. A feeling of affection or love; a fondness or liking for someone or something.

1833   Paddy Kelly's Budget 25 Sept. 277/1   Pat was about to gratify his gra for the dhrop, as well as consulting his personal safety by entering the public-house.
1841   Freemans Jrnl. 11 Aug. 3   Still I have such a gra for the good colleen..that I would lend her a few pounds to-morrow if she wanted it.
1934   P. Lynch Turf-cutter's Donkey (1988) xiv. 112   Don't cry, allanna! I've a wee bit of a gra for yerself, so tell me what mischief this Seamus is up to and I'll see about it!
1981   Irish Times 29 May 12/6   I've no gra for satire at all.
2014   Ireland's Eye Mar. 22/1   It was not something this young lad wanted to do, but as time went on he developed a grá for it and went on to win numerous dancing championships.

1833—2014(Hide quotations)
