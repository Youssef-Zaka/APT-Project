Forms:  1900s– sean nós, 1900s– sean nos, 1900s– shan nos. (Show Less)
Origin: A borrowing from Irish. Etymon: Irish sean nós.
Etymology: < Irish sean nós old custom (early 19th cent. or earlier), also specifically denoting styles of traditional musical entertainment (late 19th cent. or earlier, usually with the definite article) < sean   old (Early Irish sen  : see senior adj.) + nós   custom, manner, style (Early Irish nós  ), of uncertain origin, perhaps related to the Celtic base of Early Irish, Irish gnás   custom, practice, Welsh naws  , †gnaws   nature, disposition, itself ultimately < the same Indo-European base as know v.
The English use as modifier, as seen in sean nós singing, is not paralleled in Irish; compare e.g. Irish amhrán ar an sean nós, literally ‘song in the old style’ (expressing the same concept).
 
Parallel in Scotland.
 
Since the second half of the 20th cent., the equivalent Scottish Gaelic expression seann-nòs (18th cent. or earlier in general use) has also been used with reference to a style of singing (on the model of the Irish use), and is hence also occasionally found in English contexts.
(Show Less)
Irish English.
Categories »
 

  ‘Old custom or style.’ Used to denote a style of traditional, unaccompanied singing, usually in the Irish language. Also: used to denote a type of improvisational Irish step-dancing. Usually as a modifier, as in sean nós singing, sean nós style, etc.

1964   Irish Press 28 Jan. 11/7   Deirdre O'Connell..sings well in ‘sean nos’ style.
1970   Evening Herald (Dublin) 27 Feb. 13   The competitions include cabaret, Sean Nos singing, self-accompanied solo singing, [etc.].
1990   J. R. Cowdery Melodic Trad. Ireland i. 8   Many feel that the sean-nós is the backbone of all truly Irish music.
1996   Irish Times 27 July 24   The sean-nós concert on next Thursday will feature Donegal and Scottish sean-nós singers.
2014   Connacht Tribune 19 Sept. (T2 section) 16/1   [There are] workshops in various aspects of traditional Irish music and sean nos dancing.
