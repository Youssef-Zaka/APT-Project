Origin: A word inherited from Germanic.
Etymology: Cognate with Old Frisian gāst   (West Frisian geast  ), Old Dutch gēst  , gāst   (Middle Dutch geest  , gheest  , Dutch geest  ), Old Saxon gēst   (Middle Low German geist  , gēst  ), Old High German geist   (Middle High German geist  , German Geist  ), derivative (with dental suffix) of the same Germanic base as Old Icelandic *geiski   fear (implied in geiskafullr   full of fear), Gothic usgaisjan   to terrify, usgaisnan   to be terrified (compare gast v.1) < the same Indo-European base as Avestan zōiš- (in zōišnu shivering, trembling), and (with dental suffix) Sanskrit hīḍ- to be angry, heḍa anger, and (with additional superlative suffix) Avestan zōiždišta most terrible.
Old English.
 
The Old English form gǣst   (with i-mutation of the stem vowel) has a restricted distribution, but is frequent in some texts, including two poetic manuscripts. The currency of such a by-form with i-mutation may indicate that the word was originally an es-  , os-   stem (compare discussion of forms at rother n.).
 
Variant forms.
 
Forms with initial gh-   may show influence from Middle Dutch gheest  , a common variant of geest  . In English, such forms are first found in early printed texts of the late 15th cent. (alongside forms without -h-  ), especially in books produced by Caxton and de Worde, both of whom had connections to the Low Countries. This type of form became established gradually in the course of the 16th cent.
 
The form igaste   (in a 15th-cent. copy of an Old English charter) probably results from analogy with words in y- prefix.
 
Specific senses.
 
With many early uses compare classical Latin spīritus  spirit n., which has a similar semantic range, especially in the context of Christian religion and philosophy. In some of these senses (e.g. senses A. 2   and A. 4), spirit n.   is now more common; compare Holy Ghost n.   and Holy Spirit n.  
 
With senses A. 7   and A. 9   compare classical Latin mānes   spirits of the dead, (also) mortal remains (see manes n.).

1819   G. Samouelle Entomologist's Compend. 245   Hep[ialus] Humuli (ghost swift).
1921   Boy's Own Paper June 445/2   By night, look for the weird ghost swift moth, which appears and disappears as it flies.
2003   Brit. Jrnl. Entomol. & Nat. Hist. 16 254   Several male and female Ghost Swift Hepialus humuli (L.) came to Norman's light on the bridge as darkness fell.

1819—2003(Hide quotations)

 

Derivatives

 
 

  ghost-wise adv. in the manner of a ghost; with regard to ghosts.

1861   Portland (Maine) Transcript 16 Nov. 260/4   It was ludicrous to watch the unhappy new comers emerging ghost-wise from the misty indistinctness where no foothold was visible.
1905   Daily Chron. 16 May 4/5   He weeps, ghost-wise, at the auctioneer's bills in his bedroom.
2020   @mostly_cheese 14 Oct. in twitter.com (accessed 22 Feb. 2021)    The overwhelming majority of haunted stuff happens in hallways and stairways, which is why a studio apartment is the best choice ghost-wise.
