Forms:  1900s– fainne, 1900s– fáinne. Also with capital initial.(Show Less)
Origin: A borrowing from Irish. Etymon: Irish fáinne.
Etymology: < Irish fáinne (Early Irish fáinne  , alteration of áinne  : see note), probably ultimately related to classical Latin ānus   ring (see anus n.), although the nature of the relationship is unclear.
 
Compare earlier fawney n.
In Irish, in grammatical contexts that cause initial mutation (specifically lenition: see lenition n. 2), words beginning in a vowel are sometimes reanalysed as showing forms with initial fh- (which is silent), the lenited form of f-; this reanalysis then gives rise to a secondary initial f- in the unlenited forms (e.g. the nominative).

  A ring-shaped brooch or pin worn to indicate that the wearer is a speaker of Irish and is willing to communicate in Irish. Also as a modifier, as in fáinne badge, fáinne pin, etc.Fáinnes are usually either gold (indicating fluency) or silver (indicating a working knowledge of the language).

1919   Cork (Ireland) Examiner 15 July (City ed.) 6/8   [He said it] was very pleasing to see the extent to which Irish was spoken..by..the people of the district. The Fainne, the badge of the Irish Speakers' League, was worn very generally.
1930   Evening Herald (Dublin) 15 Aug. 5/1   Lost... Gold Fainne Pin, Thursday, North Side.
1998   A. O'Hanlon Talk of the Town (1999) i. iii. 39   After mass, I bumped into Pól O'Neadún,..fainne displayed proudly on the lapel of his tweed jacket.
2017   Belfast Tel. (Nexis) 19 Apr.   Thousands of children of my generation learnt Irish in schools.., ‘shone up our fainnes with Brasso’ and promptly forgot all we had learnt when we left school.
