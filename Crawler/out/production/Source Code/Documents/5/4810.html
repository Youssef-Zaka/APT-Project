Organization: Archive Team
Formed in 2009, the Archive Team (not to be confused with the archive.org Archive-It Team) is a rogue archivist collective dedicated to saving copies of rapidly dying or deleted websites for the sake of history and digital heritage. The group is 100% composed of volunteers and interested parties, and has expanded into a large amount of related projects for saving online and digital history.

History is littered with hundreds of conflicts over the future of a community, group, location or business that were "resolved" when one of the parties stepped ahead and destroyed what was there. With the original point of contention destroyed, the debates would fall to the wayside. Archive Team believes that by duplicated condemned data, the conversation and debate can continue, as well as the richness and insight gained by keeping the materials. Our projects have ranged in size from a single volunteer downloading the data to a small-but-critical site, to over 100 volunteers stepping forward to acquire terabytes of user-created data to save for future generations.

The main site for Archive Team is at archiveteam.org and contains up to the date information on various projects, manifestos, plans and walkthroughs.

This collection contains the output of many Archive Team projects, both ongoing and completed. Thanks to the generous providing of disk space by the Internet Archive, multi-terabyte datasets can be made available, as well as in use by the Wayback Machine, providing a path back to lost websites and work.

Our collection has grown to the point of having sub-collections for the type of data we acquire. If you are seeking to browse the contents of these collections, the Wayback Machine is the best first stop. Otherwise, you are free to dig into the stacks to see what you may find.

The Archive Team Panic Downloads are full pulldowns of currently extant websites, meant to serve as emergency backups for needed sites that are in danger of closing, or which will be missed dearly if suddenly lost due to hard drive crashes or server failures.

Hands-on teaching techniques with CELTA  

Louise Andrew and Marie Therese Swabey

Published 6 July 2021

CELTA, from Cambridge, is the most widely recognised English teaching qualification in the world, for aspiring teachers and teachers with some experience who want to develop their skills.

We’re sharing three top teaching tips as a taster of the things you will learn on a CELTA course. Just like the course, these tips are hands-on, highly practical and will help you build your confidence in the classroom.

So for success in the English language classroom, or an online teaching environment, here are our top three things to remember.

Tip 1: Engage your learners and maintain their motivation

What does this look like in practice?

  • Get to know your learners. Allow your learners to get to know you and each other by personalising learning. For example, if your aim is to learn vocabulary related to food, you might start the lesson by telling your learners about the foods you like and don’t like. You could put your learners into pairs or groups to discuss the food they like and don’t. Then, ask your learners to tell you what they have found out.
  • Use topics that are interesting for your learners. You might like to give your learners a list of topics and ask them to choose the five they like best from the list. The topics might be sport, food, travel, the environment, history, famous people and so on – whatever is interesting and relevant!
  • Give your learners some ownership of your lessons. For example, your learners might enjoy choosing a text, video or recording which you then plan a lesson around.

 

Tip 2: Minimise teacher talk, maximise learner talk

How can you make this happen?

  • Use questions. Questions require a response, which immediately increases learner talk and also helps you to check understanding. Remember to avoid too many yes/no questions.
  • Encourage your learners to take the lead. For example, if a learner says ‘I hate football’, you could ask another learner to find out why. When getting answers to a task, the learner who answers question 1 could choose another learner to answer question 2 and so on. You might also encourage learners to use the class board themselves to compare and share their ideas.
  • Avoid talking too much about what you’re going to do. For example, ‘Read the text and answer the questions in groups’ is simpler than saying: “I’m going to put you into groups of three, I’m going to give you a handout with some questions and I’m going to give you a text to read, then you’re going to read the text and answer the questions.”
  • Avoid reading out loud things which learners could read silently themselves. Instructions and questions on the board or on a handout can be read by learners and you can then ask questions to check their understanding.

 

Tip 3: Always teach language in context

What does this mean?

  • Use a context which requires the target language to be used. Context helps learners see what language means and how it is used. It makes language more relevant and memorable, and provides a vehicle for learners to come up with their own examples. Here’s an example of a context that show learners we are talking about possibilities and making plans for the future. Learners could be given a picture of two people talking, a calendar (showing today and tomorrow) and pictures of a rainy day, a sunny day, a museum, a cinema, a park, a picnic. Learners could then group the pictures – group a: rainy day, museum, cinema; group b: sunny day, park, picnic. The pictures then lead the teacher and learners to, ‘If the weather is good, we’ll go to the park’. ‘If it’s sunny, we’ll have a picnic’.
  • Avoid using random, unrelated sentences to teach new structures. For example, the teacher writes the following sentences on the board. ‘If I’m hungry, I’ll eat a banana’. ‘If the grass is long, I’ll cut it.’ ‘If I see an elephant, I’ll take a photo’. It’s more difficult to see the meaning, when things are not connected or in context.
  • Use recordings and texts with examples of target language. Recordings and texts provide a platform for learners to work out the form, meaning and use of language themselves with the help of guided questions from the teacher. Let’s take an example of someone describing the best day of their lives. This text would have multiple examples of past tenses (past simple/continuous, past perfect simple/continuous). The teacher could provide comprehension tasks and then a guided discovery task with questions focusing on the language. E.g. When did it happen? Was it a long action or a short action?

 

Invest in your teaching career

Did you know, three out of four English language teaching jobs require a CELTA qualification? Just like the tips in this article, the CELTA course covers practical teaching skills that help you to start or progress your teaching career.

If you want hands-on experience teaching real groups of learners and a certificate that will give you employment opportunities all over the world, then CELTA is the qualification for you.
