Microscopes For Kids (And Why You Need One)

Lauren Heller

Contributor

There’s an entire world around (and inside) us that’s invisible to the naked eye—the world of micro-particles and organisms. Microscopes give us a chance to take a voyeuristic peek at that tiny world. They’re an important tool for scientific research and exploration, and can be introduced to children at a young age.

Benefits for the budding scientist

Children are naturally curious creatures. Giving them hands-on activities is the perfect way to introduce them to science. It gives them the experience of scientific observation, something that can’t be appreciated through a textbook alone (especially for little ones). 

Microscopes give them insight into how the world works—just because they can’t see something doesn’t mean it’s not real. There are many invisible forces that play a part in our everyday experiences. 

“With the way the world is rapidly changing with technology (as is the workforce), we are encouraging our families to infuse STEM learning at home,” says Nadege Richards, a 3rd grade science teacher at West Philadelphia Achievement Charter Elementary School. Richards stresses the importance of STEM education for younger children. Her own family is nurturing STEM-focused curiosity through the use of microscopes, and she shared some of her favorite activities with us. 

Microscope Activities

Examine the natural world

Using a leaf as an example, a child can learn some of the investigative process of science. Like a little detective digging for answers, they can ask questions of the natural world and use a microscope to determine a conclusion.

Ask your child to discover what the structural cells of a leaf look like.

Taking a clean, dry leaf, the child will cut a cross-section and prepare it for a “wet mount” with the inner part of the leaf facing up. They can then view the cellular structure of the leaf, and record their observations in a notebook. 

The leaf experiment will lead to many more as they get curious about what a mushroom or a flower or a blade of grass look like up close. Give them freedom to explore and come up with their own questions as they examine the natural world around them. 

A conversation about germs

Germs are an abstract idea for children, but a microscope gives them a mental picture. It helps them understand the role germs play in our life (and why we wash our hands). Although they might get grossed out by what they find, exploring the world of germs and bacteria is so much fun.

Simply take a wet cotton swab and rub it on something. It could be the bathtub, the toothbrush, or the family dog. Wet a slide with the cotton swab and have a look at all the creepy crawlies that make their home with (and in) you.
