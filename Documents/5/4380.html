Coronavirus is a public health emergency, World Health Organisation declares

The World Health Organisation (WHO) has declared the new strain of coronavirus a public health emergency of international concern

Key points:

  • The WHO director-general says there has been an unprecedented outbreak
  • The biggest concern is the potential for the virus to spread to weaker health systems
  • Experts did not declare an emergency last week as they waited for evidence of person-to-person infection

It is only the sixth time WHO has declared an international emergency of this kind.

It will mean a greater focus on a worldwide response and more screening at airports.

The decision was announced after a Geneva meeting of the international organisation's emergency committee, an independent panel of 16 experts, and amid mounting evidence of the virus spreading to some 18 countries

Following WHO's announcement, Australian Prime Minister Scott Morrison said coronavirus is a "serious issue … that Australia is well equipped to deal with."

  • Everything you need to know about coronavirus in one place

China counted 9,692 confirmed cases with a death toll of 213, including 43 new fatalities on Friday.

A total of 129 cases have been reported in 22 other countries and regions, with no deaths reported outside of China.

The vast majority of the cases have been in Hubei province and its provincial capital, Wuhan, the epicentre of the outbreak.

Australia's chief medical officer, Brendan Murphy, told ABC News Breakfast that Australia has been calling for the WHO to declare a public health emergency for a number of days.

"It is a recognition of the scale of the outbreak in China and the fact that there now have been a significant number of exported cases," Mr Murphy said.

"Australia is making a substantial contribution … There is a very active group in the University of Queensland who have been involved in new vaccine development from viral viruses using molecular biology."

There are nine confirmed cases of coronavirus in Australia, Health Minister Greg Hunt said.

"The advice is that all nine patients are stable and well cared for. The first two have been released and declared post viral with a third under way," Mr Hunt said.

WHO director-general Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus said the unprecedented outbreak had been met with an unprecedented response.

"Let me be clear, this declaration is not a vote of no-confidence in China," he said.

"Our greatest concern is the potential for the virus to spread to countries with weaker health systems."

Declaration awaited evidence of human transmission

Airlines have started suspending flights to China in the wake of the outbreak, but the organisation does not recommend limiting trade and travel due to the virus outbreak.

Mr Ghebreyesus said measures taken by China to treat and contain the virus would "reverse the tide".

Twice last week the experts decided not to declare an emergency while they sought more information from China and awaited evidence of confirmed person-to-person spread of the virus in other countries, so as to meet their criteria for a global emergency.

The declaration of a global emergency triggers recommendations to all countries aimed at preventing or reducing the cross-border spread of disease, while avoiding unnecessary interference with trade and travel.

It covers temporary recommendations for national health authorities worldwide, including stepping up their monitoring, preparedness and containment measures.

Although WHO has no legal authority to sanction countries, it could ask governments to provide scientific justification for any travel or trade restrictions that they impose in the event of an international emergency.

On Friday, the US Government warned Americans not to travel to China.

A new State Department travel advisory raised the warning for China to the same level as Iraq and Afghanistan.

In a notice posted on its website, the department said: "Do not travel to China due to novel coronavirus first identified in Wuhan, China."

Fears of significant economic impact

The vast majority of the cases detected globally have been in China, where the virus originated in an illegal wildlife market in the city of Wuhan.

But nearly 100 cases have emerged in other countries, spurring cuts to travel, outbreaks of anti-China sentiment in some places and a surge in demand for protective face masks.

The total number of infections has already surpassed the total in the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) epidemic of 2002-2003.

SARS also came from China, killing about 800 people and costing the global economy an estimated $US33 billion ($49.1 billion).

Statistics from China indicate that just over 2 per cent of people known to be infected with the virus have died, suggesting that it may be less deadly than the coronaviruses behind SARS and Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS).

Economists fear the impact could be bigger this time as China now accounts for a larger share of the world economy. Markets have been spooked since news of the virus emerged earlier this month.

Cruise passengers confined to ship

About 6,000 people were prevented from leaving a cruise ship in Italy while tests for the new coronavirus were carried out on a passenger from Macau, a spokesman for the Costa Smeralda ship said.

The mayor of the Italian city of Civitavecchia said he had asked authorities to prevent passengers from disembarking until it had been confirmed there were no cases of infection.

The test carried out on the passenger proved she did not have the new coronavirus, and passengers were allowed to disembark not long after.

The 54-year-old woman, who boarded the ship on January 25 in the port of Savona, had come down with a fever and flu-like symptoms.

Both she and her partner were placed in an isolation unit on the ship.

Samples were taken to Rome's Spallanzani institute, a centre specialising in infectious diseases and viruses, which confirmed there was no infection, the health ministry added.
