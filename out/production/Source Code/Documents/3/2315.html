Airbnb outlines steps to clamp down on parties after Orinda shooting

Carolyn Said Nov. 6, 2019 Updated: Nov. 6, 2019 10:26 a.m.
Facebook Twitter Email LinkedIn Reddit Pinterest
Comments
A Contra Costa Sheriffs deputy holds the police tape up for investigators to drive under at the intersection of Knickerbocker Lane and Spring Road near the location where several people were shot at a Halloween party on Lucille Way in Orinda , Calif., on Friday, November 1, 2019. Early reports stated that 4 people had been killed with others injured and transported to nearby hospitals. Though several of the injuries were believed to have been caused when guests ran from the shooting.
Photo: Carlos Avila Gonzalez / The Chronicle

Airbnb took steps Wednesday to clamp down on unauthorized house parties, in the wake of shootings at a Halloween party at an Orinda Airbnb rental that caused five deaths.

Trust is vital to the short-term rental platform, wrote Airbnb CEO and co-founder Brian Chesky in an email to employees. “People need to feel like they can trust our community, and that they can trust Airbnb when something does go wrong.”

He outlined several specific changes:

>Verify listings. Airbnb will start Wednesday to verify the accuracy of all 7 million listings for home and room rentals on its site. It expects to be finished by Dec. 15, 2020, he said. “Homes will be verified for accuracy of the listing (including accuracy of photos, addresses, and listing details) and quality standards (including cleanliness, safety, and basic home amenities) and those that meet our high expectations will be clearly labeled.”

>Guest guarantee. Starting Dec. 15 of this year, guests who find a listing does not match its description can get rebooked into an equivalent property or get a 100% refund.

>Neighbor hotline. A new 24/7 hotline to be introduced in the U.S. by the end of the year will be staffed with a rapid response team to answer concerns by neighbors. The number will be made prominently available. Airbnb has hired two advisers to help start it: Charles Ramsey, former chief of police in Philadelphia and Washington, D.C., and Ronald Davis, former chief of police in East Palo Alto and executive director of community-oriented policing services in the Obama administration.
