pay gap, n.

falsefalse

Pronunciation: 
Brit. Hear pronunciation/ˈpeɪ ɡap/
, 
U.S. Hear pronunciation/ˈpeɪ ˌɡæp/
Origin: Formed within English, by compounding. Etymons: pay n., gap n.1
Etymology: < pay n. + gap n.1(Show Less)
 

 1. A difference in pay between two sectors of a population.Pay gaps can be measured in various ways. The term is typically used in contexts where one group of people earns more on average than another and this is considered undesirable or unfair.

1948   Bath (Maine) Independent 25 Mar. 6/4   A definite tendency to lessen the pay gap between men and women teachers.
1992   Economist 3 Oct. 31/2   The pay gap between the lowest and average earners is now the widest since records began in 1886, according to the Low Pay Unit. The gap will increase if Wages Councils are abolished, it said.
2020   Dorset Echo (Nexis) 16 Oct.   NHS workers are to stage a protest in Dorchester to draw attention to their claims for pay justice... ‘In the last 10 years the pay gap between MPs and health workers has really grown.’

1948—2020(Hide quotations)

 
 

 2.   gender pay gap   n. (chiefly with the) a difference in pay between men and women.A gender pay gap can be measured in various ways. The term is typically used in contexts where men earn more on average than women and this is considered undesirable or unfair.

1984   Colorado Springs Gaz. Tel. 8 Apr. f6/1   To feminists the quickest way to bridge the gender pay gap is simply to raise the pay of ‘pink-collar’ jobs.
2017   Australian (Nexis) 5 Dec. (Commentary section) 12   In terms of the overall gender pay gap, what the Harvard research indicates is the importance of the wage premium attached to long and unpredictable hours of work.

1984—2017(Hide quotations)
