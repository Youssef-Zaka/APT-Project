Origin: Apparently a variant or alteration of another lexical item. Etymon: cod walloper n.
Etymology: Apparently an alteration of cod walloper n. (compare sense 3   at that entry), probably after trollop n. (compare also dollop n. 2b, lollop n. 2).
In sense 2   probably originally a euphemistic substitution for a stronger expression. Compare note at sense 2, and compare also remarks by the authors of the earliest attested instances in this sense, made in a 2005 interview carried out for the television programme Balderdash and Piffle:
2005   A. Simpson in Transcript of Interview by V. Coren with R. Galton & A. Simpson (Takeaway Media) (O.E.D. Archive) 3   As far as I was concerned, it [sc. the word ‘codswallop’] was just a funny sounding name and when we used it in Hancock,..it was instead of saying ‘what a load of old rubbish’.
2005   R. Galton in Transcript of Interview by V. Coren with R. Galton & A. Simpson (Takeaway Media) (O.E.D. Archive) 4   It could be a euphemism for a, for a word that you couldn't say in, on the BBC in those days, like bollocks, what a load of old bollocks..or cobblers, bollocks, anything like that, so it could be a word that erm, is acceptable in polite company.
It is unclear whether the following instance (punningly after place names like Nether Wallop  , Over Wallop  , Hampshire) reflects sense 1   or implies earlier currency of sense 2:
1958   R. Larter (title of pencil sketch)    Welcome to Codswallop.
(Show Less)
slang (chiefly British and Australian).
Categories »
 

 1. British slang (depreciative, chiefly London). An overly talkative woman, a gossip. Also in more general use, as a mildly depreciative term for a person. Now rare.In quot. 2005   in historical use.

1928   News of World 12 Feb. 11/2   What is a ‘cod's wallop’? According to a learned counsel..the term is an East-end colloquialism for ‘a woman who cannot keep her mouth shut’.
1958   Alton (Illinois) Evening Tel. 7 July 4/4   Maurice Arthur Fish, a Bournemouth, England, butcher, has changed his name to Carrington-Fisher. Said his wife: ‘We've been called Fishy, Kipper and Codswallop.’
2005   A. Simpson in Transcript of Interview by V. Coren with R. Galton & A. Simpson (Takeaway Media) (O.E.D. Archive) 2   In the thirties,..I was about seven or eight and my uncle..used to use it as a proper noun, he used to call me codswallop.

1928—2005(Hide quotations)

 
Thesaurus »
Categories »
 

 2. Nonsense, rubbish, drivel. Often in a load of (old) codswallop.Popularized in this sense, which they may have coined, by writers Alan Simpson (1929–2017) and Ray Galton (1930–2018), who used it as a euphemism for a stronger expression (e.g. cobblers, bollocks) in the British comedy series Hancock's Half Hour and Citizen James in the late 1950s and early 1960s, chiefly in dialogue delivered by actor Sid James. See note in etymology, and cf. quot. 2005 at sense 1.

1959   A. Simpson & R. Galton Two Murderers (B.B.C. TV camera script) in Hancock's Half Hour 5th Ser. Episode 2. 7   Sidney. You were counting your money weren't you. Tony. I was not. Sidney. Don't give me that old codswallop, you were counting your money.
1960   A. Simpson & R. Galton Hancock's Half Hour (B.B.C. TV camera script: 6th Ser. Episode 2) 21   Sid. Well..if that's reading, you can stick it. What a load of old codswallop. The Stranglers of Bolton. They should have strangled the bloke who wrote it.
1963   Radio Times 17 Oct. 52/2   Just branding a programme as ‘rubbish’, ‘tripe’, or—there are a lot of these—‘codswallop’, gives little indication of what moved the viewer to write.
1970   Peace News 30 Jan. 6/1   I hope you will stop publishing cod's wallop of the type which appeared..in Roger Moody's article.
1998   J. Holms Bad Vibes xiii. 168   If Gloria was telling the truth it was certainly not the whole truth and the likelihood was that her whole story was a load of complete codswallop.
2018   Birmingham Evening Mail (Nexis) 1 June   What a load of codswallop the obesity crisis is.

1959—2018(Hide quotations)
