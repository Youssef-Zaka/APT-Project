 b. Esp. in the western United States: a man or boy who herds cattle on an open range or (later) a ranch, usually working on horseback. Later also: a character type in the genre of the Western (see western n.2 4) and more widely in popular culture, based (often somewhat loosely) on such herdsmen and ranchers and typically characterized as a skilled horseman and gunfighter. Cf. cowgirl n. 2.This sense developed as a specific use of sense 1, probably significantly influenced also by vaquero n.  

 
Cowboys herded cattle on the Great Plains in the days of open ranges in the second half of the 19th cent. As the cattle industry spread northward from Texas into Canada and westward into the Rocky Mountains, they were frequently employed to drive large herds to new pastures. In the late 19th cent. most cowboys began to take more settled work on ranches as the introduction of barbed wire made it cheaper and easier to enclose previously open ranges, and railway expansion made trail-driving increasingly uneconomical. However, the idea of cowboys leading largely nomadic lives remained an important part of the cultural stereotype and informed many of the extended uses at branch II.See also Cowboys and Indians at Phrases.
