vaccinologist, n.

Pronunciation: 
Brit. Hear pronunciation/ˌvaksᵻˈnɒlədʒɪst/
, 
U.S. Hear pronunciation/ˌvæksəˈnɑlədʒəst/
Origin: Formed within English, by derivation. Etymons: vaccine n., -ologist comb. form.
Etymology: < vaccine n. + -ologist comb. form.
 
Compare later vaccinology n.(Show Less)
Categories »
 

  Originally: a person who performs or is experienced in or knowledgeable about vaccination, esp. against smallpox. In later use more generally: an expert or specialist in the branch of biomedical science dealing with the study of immunization and the development, production, and use of vaccines. Cf. vaccinology n.

1808   Obstetric Ejaculations on Cow Pock xi. 27   This manie [sc. une manie humaine] perhaps is more implicated than truth, in the war with the vaccinologists.
1868   Boston Med. & Surg. Jrnl. 77 139   There is a general agreement among vaccinologists as to what constitutes a ‘good’ vaccinal cicatrix.
1907   Brit. Med. Jrnl. 14 Sept. 662/2   The author of the article on Vaccination in..the Encyclopaedia Britannica was a pathologist rather than a vaccinologist familiar with parasitology and bacteriology.
1990   Epidemiol. & Infection 102 268   Some vaccinologists and regulatory authorities consider that a randomized, blind, placebo-controlled trial is essential to establish the protective efficacy of any vaccine.
2021   New Yorker 4 Jan. 28/2   Until recently, one of the main imaging tools used by vaccinologists, the cryogenic electron microscope, wasn't powerful enough to visualize viral proteins, which are incredibly tiny.
