U.S. Hear pronunciation/ˈfɑdə/
, 
Irish English /ˈfɑðə/
Origin: Formed within English, by clipping or shortening. Etymon: síneadh fada n.
Etymology: Shortened < síneadh fada n.
The following earlier passage apparently cites the Irish adjective fada   as if it was a noun, either erroneously or as a (spontaneous) shortening of síneadh fada  síneadh fada n.   in an English context:
1939   Fermanagh Herald 30 Dec. 2   Accentuation is on the syllable of the word that bears a dash above it—what is called in Irish a fada.
 
The usual shortening of the phrase within Irish is síneadh.
(Show Less)
Thesaurus »
 

  An Irish diacritic mark having the form of an acute accent, which is placed over a vowel to indicate that the letter should be pronounced with a long sound; = síneadh fada n.

1981   Irish Times 2 July (City ed.) 9/4   Since 1953, many stamps have borne the word ‘Eire’... The name of Ireland is Éire, and spelt without the fada, the word means a burden or an encumbrance.
1996   Australian (Nexis) 9 July (Local section) 3   The O'Briens..say they are facing a battle..after the Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade last week refused to recognise the fada on Ciarán's passport.
2012   Irish Times (Nexis) 18 May 11   A motion from the Newcastle West branch [of Sinn Féin] in Co Limerick regrets the absence of the fada in many official publications and urges Sinn Féin to set an example in its correct use.
