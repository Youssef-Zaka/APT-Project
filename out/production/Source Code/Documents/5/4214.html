, 
U.S. Hear pronunciation/ˈæbəˌɡeɪl/
Forms:  also with capital initial.(Show Less)
Frequency (in current use):  Show frequency band information
Origin: From a proper name. Etymon: proper name Abigail.
Etymology: < Abigail (Hebrew 'aḇīgayil), the name of a Carmelite woman, the wife of Nabal (and later of King David) in the Old Testament and Hebrew Scriptures (1 Samuel 25:3 ff.), who impressed King David with her humility and helpfulness, putting herself in danger in order to be of service to him; in conversation with him, she frequently refers to herself as ‘thine handmaid’ (1 Samuel 25:24–31); probably popularized by its use as the name of a female servant in F. Beaumont & J. Fletcher Scornful Ladie (1616).(Show Less)
Now archaic and historical.

  A lady's maid; a female servant or attendant.

1616   Bp. W. Cowper Two Fruitfull Treat. ii. Ep. Ded. sig. L   Through his grace yee haue beene an Abigail to that house, whereunto your God did bring you, that is, a mother to it.
1664   T. Killigrew Parsons Wedding ii. vi, in Comedies & Trag. 99   And ugly, her Abigail, she had her say too?
1693   W. Congreve Old Batchelour iii. i. 22   Thou art some forsaken Abigail, we have dallied with heretofore.
1733   E. Phillips Mock Lawyer i. 19   A Coquet is the same Creature from a Coronet to an Abigail.
1771   T. Smollett Humphry Clinker I. 98   An antiquated Abigail, dressed in her lady's cast-clothes.
1784   W. Hayley Mausoleum iii. i, in Plays 415   To an Abigail's room did the old Bard repair?
1809   B. H. Malkin tr. A. R. Le Sage Adventures Gil Blas II. iv. vii. 153   An old abigail, whom I had formerly known as maid of all work to an actress?
1849   E. Bulwer-Lytton Caxtons III. xiv. vi. 41   The woman..was dressed with a quiet neatness, that seemed to stamp her profession as that of an abigail.
1864   Duke of Manchester Court & Society I. 81   Her house remained full of dons and pages, ladies and abigails.
1932   G. Heyer Devil's Cub xviii. 283   He asked warily whether her abigail was outside, with her baggage.
1987   J. Staynes & M. Storey Goodbye, Nanny Gray vi. 72   One door recess had been widened to allow one solid, oak, polished chair, in consideration for the abigail or footman who waited a life away..for the bell within.

1616—1987(Hide quotations)
