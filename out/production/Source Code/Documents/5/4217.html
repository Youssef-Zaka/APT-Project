  An inn or alehouse. Now historical.Probably with reference to the changing of horses at inns.

a1653   Z. Boyd Zion's Flowers (1855) 72   When men see the Ivy bush hang out, They knowe the change-house.
1679   T. Kirke Mod. Acct. Scotl. 15   The High-ways in Scotland are tolerably good..; they have not Inns, but Change houses (as they call them) poor small Cottages, where you must be content to take what you find.
a1694   A. Balfour Lett. (1700) ii. 52   A little kind of chainge-house..that provides Meat for Men and Horses.
1741   Case Settlem. of Parish of Fearn 6   Having spent the greatest Part of the Day..in drinking in different Change-houses one after another.
1777   Whole Proc. Jockey & Maggy (rev. ed.) 7   At every change house they chanced to pass by, providence stopt their proceeding, with full stoups, bottles and glasses drinking their healths.
1814   W. Scott Waverley I. xi. 145   The guests had left their horses at the small inn, or change-house, as it was called, of the village.  View more context for this quotation
1848   A. H. Clough Bothie of Toper-na-Fuosich vi. 83   These..Went by the lochside along to the changehouse near in the clachan.
1901   W. J. Milne Reminisc. Old Boy 289   [He] hied him ower tae the cheenge-hoose o' Wat Wabblestraucht.
1992   D. Purves Shakespeare's Tragedie o Macbeth iii. iv. 33   Even a chynge-houss denner is better hansilt.
2000   J. Burnett Riot, Revelry & Rout vi. 118   Country races were organised by change-house keepers.
