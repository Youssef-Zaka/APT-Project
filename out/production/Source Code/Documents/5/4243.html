† narel, n.

falsefalse

$Revision$
Forms:  late Middle English narel, late Middle English narellis (plural), 1600s narell, 1600s narrell. (Show Less)
Origin: Apparently a borrowing from French.
Etymology: Apparently < an Anglo-Norman or Middle French derivative of classical Latin nāris  nare n.; compare -el suffix2. Compare Anglo-Norman nareille  , nariel  , naril  , narile  , narille  , narrille   and Middle French narille   (early 12th cent. in Old French; also in Old French as narile  ; French †nareau  ). Compare norril n.
For an isolated borrowing of a Middle French plural form compare:
a1450   tr. Guy de Chauliac Grande Chirurgie (Caius 336/725) (1970) 24   Of apostumatis of þe eeren & narilx & of polipo & vlceris of þe nose schal be seid in de vlceribus.
(Show Less)
Obsolete. rare.

  A nostril. Cf. nare n.

1486   Bk. St. Albans sig. cvjv   Brynne the Narellis thourogh owte.
1611   R. Cotgrave Dict. French & Eng. Tongues   Canolles, a haukes Narell; one of the little holes whereat she draws in, and lets out, her breath.
