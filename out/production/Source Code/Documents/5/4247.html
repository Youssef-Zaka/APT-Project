† metamorphosy, n.

falsefalse

  • Full entry

$Revision$
Forms:  1500s–1600s metamorphosie, 1500s–1600s metamorphosy, 1600s metamorphisie. (Show Less)
Origin: A borrowing from Latin. Etymon: Latin metamorphōsis.
Etymology: < classical Latin metamorphōsis metamorphosis n., with alteration of ending (compare -y suffix3). Compare earlier metamorphosis n.(Show Less)
Obsolete.
Thesaurus »
 

  A tale in which people, animals, etc., are changed in form. Also: a change in form or nature; the result of this; metamorphosis.

1532   L. Cox Art or Crafte Rhetoryke sig. D.viv   And so dothe Ulysses conclude his oracion in the .xiii. boke of Ouides metamorphosy.
?1573   H. Cheke tr. F. Negri Freewyl iii. iii. 115   These cities were changed into more strange Metamorphosies, then the Metamorphosis of Ouide.
1606   W. Warner Continuance Albions Eng. xiv. lxxxix. 363   Like tales and Metamorphosies passe many in this Chat.
1664   London Prodigal 16   Nere muse man at this metamorphosie.
a1698   W. Temple Poems 46   If ever any reasonable Soul Harbor'd in shape of either brute or fowl, This was the Mansion: Metamorphosie Gain'd here the credit lost in Poetrie.

1532—a1698(Hide quotations)
